# The broken rung — promotion from IC to first management

*Week 6 report — 2026-06-15*

## Executive summary

In technical roles, only **52 women are promoted to manager for every 100 men** — barely half the all-industry rate of 86 (McKinsey/LeanIn *Women in the Workplace 2021*; still the most recent technical-roles cut). The all-industry broken rung *did* finally move in 2025 — **93 women promoted per 100 men, up from 81 in 2024** — but the gain was selective: **women of color stayed at 74 per 100, and Black women at 60, unchanged**. The broken rung is the single mechanism behind every senior-level gap this knowledge base has logged; it is now repairing for some women and not others, and **no source has measured it inside AI specifically at all.**

## 1. What's the number?

The "broken rung" is McKinsey and LeanIn's name for the first promotion from individual contributor to manager — the step where the pipeline first leaks, and the one that determines everything above it. The denominator matters: this is **promotions to first-level manager per 100 men promoted at the same step**, across a US corporate-pipeline dataset (the 2025 survey component covers **120+ companies and ~9,000 employees**; the broader pipeline tracks millions). Their working definition of "women" is self-identified gender; the headline numbers are not gender-diverse-inclusive beyond that.

The defining number for *this* topic is not the all-industry figure — it is the **technical-roles** cut: **52 women promoted to manager for every 100 men**, against 86 across all roles and industries (McKinsey/LeanIn *Women in the Workplace 2021*). In technical functions the rung is roughly **twice as broken** as the corporate average. That 2021 figure is the most recent technical-specific number McKinsey has published; the gap has not been refreshed since, which is itself a finding (see §3). `[dated]`

The all-industry rung, by contrast, is freshly measured and — for the first time in this knowledge base — **moved in the right direction**:

| Measure (per 100 men promoted to manager) | Women | Source |
|---|---|---|
| All industries, 2024 | **81** | WitW 2024 |
| All industries, 2025 | **93** | WitW 2025 |
| Women of color, 2025 | **74** | WitW 2025 |
| Asian women, 2025 | 82 | WitW 2025 |
| Latinas, 2025 | 82 | WitW 2025 |
| Black women, 2025 | **60** | WitW 2025 |
| **Technical roles (all women), 2021** | **52** | WitW 2021 `[dated]` |

The 81→93 jump looks like genuine progress. But disaggregating kills the celebration: **women of color sat at 74 per 100 in 2025, and Black women at 60 — both essentially unchanged from 2024.** The all-women average rose because white women's promotion rate rose. The rung is not repairing; it is **repairing selectively.**

The representation pyramid shows the compounding cost. Women are **48% of entry-level workers but 39% of managers and just 29% of the C-suite** (2025, C-suite unchanged from 2024). In STEM functions the leak is steeper still: **29% entry-level → 24.4% managerial → 12.2% C-suite** (2024, Women in Tech Network analysis). A field that starts at near-parity and ends near one-in-eight did most of its leaking at the very first rung.

## 2. What's the trend?

**The all-industry rung, recent series (promotions to manager per 100 men):**

| Year | Women per 100 men | Source |
|---|---|---|
| 2021 | 86 (all roles) / **52 (technical)** | WitW 2021 |
| 2023 | 87 | WitW 2023 |
| 2024 | 81 | WitW 2024 |
| 2025 | **93** | WitW 2025 |

*(Note: across years McKinsey has reported the rung two ways — promotions-only, and hired-plus-promoted. The series above is the promotions-to-manager metric where stated; the hired-plus-promoted framing in earlier reports ran lower. The 2024→2025 comparison is like-for-like.)* `[methodology gap]`

The one-sentence read: **improving on average, stalled for women of color, and roughly twice as bad in technical roles where it has not been measured in four years.** For the 11th consecutive year, women remain underrepresented at every level of the pipeline.

**Why the rung — and not the glass ceiling — is the binding constraint.** Because men outnumber women at the first manager level, there are structurally fewer women in the pool eligible for every subsequent promotion. McKinsey's own modeling is blunt: fix hiring parity at entry and nothing else, and the pipeline still never reaches parity, because the rung re-introduces the deficit every year. This is the mechanism behind the **<14% women in senior executive AI roles** (Interface EU 2024) and the **16.1% women on tenure-track AI faculty** (Stanford HAI 2024) that earlier reports documented — and behind the executive-level **$0.69-per-$1.00 pay cliff** in Week 5. The pay cliff at the top is, in large part, a promotion cliff at the bottom.

**The AI accelerant.** Two 2025 findings suggest the rung may *widen* in AI specifically before it narrows. Entry-level women are told to use AI tools by their managers far less than men (**21% vs. 33%**, WitW 2025, covered in Week 4), and men use AI daily at a rate about **22% higher** than women (LeanIn / WEF 2025). If AI fluency becomes a promotion criterion — and in AI-building organizations it will — an unequal head start at the IC level converts directly into an unequal promotion rate at the rung.

## 3. What's missing?

1. **No AI-specific broken-rung number exists.** The closest proxy is the 2021 "technical roles" figure (52 per 100), which bundles all engineering and product functions, is four years old, and predates the generative-AI hiring boom entirely. **No source has published a manager-promotion rate by gender for AI engineers, ML scientists, or research staff.** This is the central gap of this topic.

2. **The technical-roles cut has not been refreshed since 2021.** McKinsey's all-industry rung is annual; the technical breakout is not. We cannot say whether the technical rung tracked the all-industry improvement (81→93) or stayed stuck.

3. **No intersectional (race × gender) promotion data inside AI or technical roles.** We have women-of-color rung numbers for the whole economy (74; Black women 60) and technical-roles numbers for all women (52) — but never the two crossed. The Black-woman-in-AI promotion rate is unmeasured at every level.

4. **No named AI lab publishes first-line-manager representation or promotion rates by gender.** Diversity reports (Google, Microsoft) give technical-workforce headcount but not promotion velocity. The residency and IC data gaps logged in Weeks 1 and 4 extend cleanly to management.

5. **"Manager" is defined inconsistently.** In IC-heavy AI orgs, the first rung is often a *staff/principal* IC promotion, not a people-management move. No dataset distinguishes the technical-IC ladder from the management ladder by gender — so the AI-relevant rung may not even be the one McKinsey measures.

6. **Sponsorship is measured as access, not as outcome.** We know 31% of entry-level women have a sponsor vs. 45% of men (2025), but no public dataset links a named sponsorship program to a measured change in *women's* promotion rate at the rung specifically.

## 4. What works?

**Structured, debiased promotion processes — the strongest signal.** In McKinsey's technical-roles study, of 40 leaders interviewed, **only 2 reported organizations without a broken-rung problem — both Fortune 100 tech companies with heavily structured, committee-based promotion systems** (defined skill criteria, manager calibration, integrated bias checks, decisions made by panels rather than individual managers). The mechanism is the finding: discretion at the rung is where bias enters; removing discretion is what closes it.

**The three enablers (McKinsey/LeanIn technical-roles framework):**
- **Equitable access to skill-building and high-visibility projects** — the assignments that make someone "obviously ready" for management.
- **Structured promotion processes** — transparent criteria, calibration, committee decisions, bias checks.
- **A sponsorship culture** — not mentorship (advice) but sponsorship (advocacy in the room where promotions are decided).

**Sponsorship parity closes the ambition gap — measured.** Women want promotions nearly as much as men (**80% vs. 86%**, 2025) yet are 13% less likely to receive one. LeanIn's 2025 finding is the actionable one: **when women receive equal career support, the gap in promotion ambition disappears entirely.** The deficit is not ambition; it is sponsorship — and sponsorship is the most directly fixable of the three enablers.

**The business case is quantified, not aspirational.** Companies with the most gender-diverse leadership are **48% more likely to financially outperform** the least diverse (McKinsey *Diversity Matters Even More*, 2023). The rung is not only an equity problem; it is a measured performance cost.

*Untested but frequently cited:* manager-bias training (rarely tied to a promotion-rate change), "returnship" programs (re-entry, not the rung), and ERG-based mentorship (advice without advocacy). Each is plausible; none has published a comparison-group effect on the rung itself.

## 5. Recommended actions

1. **Read** — McKinsey/LeanIn's *Repairing the broken rung on the career ladder for women in technical roles*. It is the only source that isolates the technical rung (52 per 100) and names the three enablers with a clear mechanism. Pair it with the *Women in the Workplace 2025* key findings for the fresh all-industry numbers.

2. **Share** — The split statistic: **the rung rose to 93 per 100 overall in 2025, but women of color stayed at 74 and Black women at 60.** It is the most economical way to puncture "the broken rung is fixing itself" — it is fixing for some women and not others.

3. **Ask** — Of your org or board: *"What is our promotion rate to first-level manager, by gender and race, for technical roles specifically — and is the promotion decision made by a calibrated committee or by individual managers?"* If the rate isn't tracked or the decision is discretionary, that is the answer.

4. **Track** — The annual **Women in the Workplace** release (typically Q3/Q4) for the all-industry rung, and watch for any refresh of the **technical-roles cut** — the single number this knowledge base most needs updated.

5. **Act** — If you are senior enough to sit in a promotion calibration, **sponsor, don't mentor**: name a specific woman IC and advocate for her readiness in the room where the decision is made. Sponsorship parity is the one enabler shown to erase the ambition gap outright.

## Methodology notes for this report

**Two metrics, one name.** McKinsey has reported the "broken rung" both as promotions-to-manager-per-100-men and as hired-plus-promoted-per-100-men; the numbers differ and coverage often conflates them. Earlier WAI reports cited the 2024 hired-plus-promoted-adjacent figure (81). This report uses the promotions-to-manager metric for the 2024→2025 comparison (81→93) and flags the technical-roles figure (52, 2021) as the topic-defining but dated number.

**The headline improvement is real but partial.** The 81→93 gain is genuine and like-for-like, but it is an average that conceals a flat result for women of color (74) and Black women (60). Any single-number summary of the broken rung in 2025 is misleading; the disaggregation *is* the story.

**The central evidence gap** — no AI-specific or even refreshed technical-roles rung — is now logged for the dashboard.
