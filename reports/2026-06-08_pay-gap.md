# The pay gap in AI and machine learning roles

*Week 5 report — 2026-06-08*

## Executive summary

Women in US computer-and-mathematical occupations earn **$0.80 per $1.00** of men's median weekly earnings (BLS 2024, uncontrolled) — a 20-cent uncontrolled gap. The more useful statistic is the **controlled vs. uncontrolled split**: for the Computer & Mathematical group Payscale 2024 reports **$0.92 uncontrolled / $0.97 controlled**. The single most underreported finding is from **Carta's Gap Table**: women are **35% of equity-holding tech employees but hold only 20% of equity value** — a 5-to-1 underweight. Cash gaps are smaller and have narrowed; **equity gaps remain unaddressed and are where lifetime AI compensation is actually decided.**

## 1. What's the number?

The single number that hits most readers is **$0.80 per $1.00** — women's median weekly earnings in US Computer & Mathematical occupations, full-time wage and salary workers (BLS *Highlights of Women's Earnings 2024*). This is **uncontrolled**: it does not adjust for role, level, experience, location, or hours. The split tells more:

| Measure | Women's $ per men's $1 | Source |
|---|---|---|
| US Computer & Mathematical occupations (uncontrolled) | $0.80 | BLS 2024 |
| US Computer & Mathematical occupations (uncontrolled) | $0.92 | Payscale 2024 |
| US Computer & Mathematical occupations (controlled — same job, level, experience, location) | $0.97 | Payscale 2024 |
| All US workers, median hourly (uncontrolled) | $0.85 | Pew Research 2024 |

The discrepancy between BLS (0.80) and Payscale (0.92) on the same group is methodological: BLS uses CPS median weekly earnings with no controls; Payscale uses a different sample (its compensation platform) with hours and structure normalization. **Both numbers are correct for what they measure.** The controlled $0.97 is the residual gap after observable adjustments — the part the field still cannot explain.

**No primary source publishes a clean "AI engineer" or "data scientist" pay gap specifically.** `[methodology gap]` BLS does not yet have a separate SOC code for AI engineers; Payscale and Levels.fyi do not publish AI-only cuts. The headline figures here treat the broader Computer & Mathematical SOC group as the working proxy.

## 2. What's the trend?

**By level** (Payscale 2026 across all industries):
- Individual contributor (uncontrolled): $0.83 / (controlled): $1.00
- Manager: $0.83 / $0.95
- Director: $0.82 / $0.93
- **Executive: $0.69 / $0.92** — collapses at the very top.

The controlled gap **widens with seniority**. The uncontrolled gap is roughly constant IC-through-Director, then drops sharply at the executive level. The Levels.fyi Q1 2024 report (17,116 US software-engineering submissions; 22% women at entry, 13% at senior) describes the same pattern qualitatively: the gap "gets more pronounced at higher levels in the US," varying by metro — Bay Area widens at senior; Seattle/NYC largest at L4. Levels.fyi publishes box plots but withholds exact % gaps. `[methodology gap]`

**Direction over time:** Pew Research finds the US overall pay gap has narrowed only slightly over 20 years. The Colorado pay-transparency law (effective Jan 2021) is the cleanest natural experiment: state gap narrowed from 78¢ (2021) to 85¢ (Feb 2024) to **86¢ (Jul 2025)** per Women's Foundation of Colorado / Tejara — a 7-point gain in three and a half years that does *not* show up at comparable speed in BLS national data.

Cullen (*Journal of Economic Perspectives*, 2024, "Is Pay Transparency Good?") finds posted salaries rose +3.6% on average; horizontal transparency can compress pay downward. Arnold/Quadlin and others: salary-info job postings rose ~30 pp after transparency laws. Transparency moves the number; the direction of the wage effect is mixed.

## 3. What's missing?

1. **No primary source publishes the gender pay gap isolated to AI/ML engineers or research scientists.** BLS does not yet have a separate SOC code; Payscale and Levels.fyi do not publish AI-only cuts.

2. **No intersectional (Black women, Latina women) pay data inside AI/ML roles.** All available numbers are all-occupation. The gap between Black women ($0.63) and white non-Hispanic men, and Latinas ($0.54–$0.58), is well-documented in aggregate (NWLC / IWPR 2024) — but never in AI specifically.

3. **No equity-vs-cash decomposition by gender for AI roles specifically.** Carta's Gap Table is whole-tech; Levels.fyi does not split. Given that equity is where AI compensation actually concentrates (research scientist offers at major labs are 60–80% equity), **this is the largest single under-measured component of women's AI pay.**

4. **Levels.fyi Q1 2024 report shows charts but withholds exact %-gap numbers by level.** Researchers must estimate from box plots.

5. **No India-specific AI/ML gender pay number** from NASSCOM or Monster (NASSCOM 2024 compensation report covers AI/ML premiums but not by gender; full microdata is paywalled).

6. **No lifetime earnings projection specific to AI careers.** The NWLC 40-year cumulative figures ($430,480 average; $877,480 Black women; $1,007,080 Latinas) are all-occupation. The AI-specific version would be materially larger because AI compensation is higher and more equity-weighted than the average.

## 4. What works?

**Single-firm measured intervention:**

- **Salesforce equal-pay audits** (annual, since 2015). Cumulative ~**$10.3M spent** on adjustments by 2019; **$5.6M in 2022 alone** on unexplained pay differences globally. In 2024 the company began publishing adjusted pay ratios by gender and race. The lesson is mechanical: a one-time audit catches accumulated gaps; an *annualized* audit prevents new gaps from forming. Salesforce is the only tech company with a documented multi-year audit-and-correct record.

**Policy intervention with measured outcome:**

- **Colorado Equal Pay for Equal Work Act** (effective Jan 2021). State pay gap 78¢ (2021) → 86¢ (Jul 2025) per Tejara/Women's Foundation analysis. Cullen (2024) finds posted salaries rose +3.6% on average and horizontal transparency can compress pay downward. Salary-info job postings rose ~30 pp post-law per Arnold et al.

**Negotiation gap — newly reframed:**

- The "women don't ask" narrative is increasingly contradicted by current evidence. Vanderbilt / Kennedy (2023) finds women now negotiate as often or more often than men. Pew Research (April 2023): most US workers — both sexes — do not negotiate starting pay; men slightly more likely to. Glassdoor's older finding (men 3× more likely to negotiate a raise) reflects an earlier cohort. **Net: negotiation differences explain a small and shrinking share of the gap; most of the gap is offered, not asked.**

**Equity gap — surfacing but unaddressed:**

- Carta's Gap Table (~6,000 cap tables; ~$45B equity value) is the cleanest evidence of the equity-vs-cash decomposition. Women = 35% of equity-holding employees but hold **20% of employee equity value**; women founders hold 6% of founder equity vs. 13% of founder headcount. **No tech company has published an equity-equity-audit equivalent of Salesforce's cash-pay audit.**

## 5. Recommended actions

1. **Read** — Carta's **Gap Table** report. It is the single best evidence base for the cash-vs-equity decomposition and the strongest argument for treating equity-equity as a distinct problem from pay-equity.

2. **Share** — The **35% equity-holders / 20% equity value** statistic. It is the most economical way to puncture "tech pay is roughly fair now" — the cash gap has narrowed; the equity gap is wider and far less discussed.

3. **Ask** — Of your employer, mentor, or company you're advising: *"What is our equity-equity audit, separately from our cash-pay audit?"* If the answer is "we don't do one," that is the answer.

4. **Track** — The **annual BLS Highlights of Women's Earnings** release (typically Q3). Computer & Mathematical occupational gap is the longest-running consistent series; watch the controlled vs. uncontrolled split where Payscale publishes it.

5. **Act** — If you are negotiating, **negotiate the equity component explicitly**, not just base salary. The literature shows equity is where the gap is widest, and the equity gap is the one most likely to be silently agreed to.

## Methodology notes for this report

**Always specify controlled vs. uncontrolled.** Mixing them is the single most common error in pay-gap coverage. Uncontrolled gaps are the lived-experience number (what a woman actually takes home vs. a man). Controlled gaps are the residual after structural factors — useful for diagnosing discrimination separately from segregation/level mix.

The BLS 0.80 and Payscale 0.92 numbers for the same occupational group are not contradictory; they reflect different samples and different normalizations. Both are correct.

The Carta equity finding is the most under-discussed statistic in the women-in-AI pay literature. Any future report should chase whether any AI lab has audited equity-equity by gender (none has, publicly, as of this writing).
