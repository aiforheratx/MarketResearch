# Entry-level AI roles: first job in AI/ML

*Week 4 report — 2026-06-01*

## Executive summary

The most striking new finding in 2025 is from McKinsey/LeanIn's *Women in the Workplace 2025*: **entry-level women report 21% manager encouragement to use AI tools vs. 33% for men** — a 12-point gap at the very moment when AI fluency is becoming a promotion lever. The classic "broken rung" persists too: **for every 100 men hired or promoted into entry-level-or-manager roles, only 81 women are** (McKinsey 2024). And the **most-cited entry-level women-in-tech retention statistic — 50% of women in tech leave by age 35 (AnitaB.org)** — has *no AI-specific equivalent* in any public dataset. The data we have about women's first AI jobs is dominated by what we do not measure.

## 1. What's the number?

**Women's share of global AI engineering talent: 30.5% in 2024**, up from 29.3% in 2016 (LinkedIn Economic Graph via OECD.AI). This measures self-reported AI engineering skills among LinkedIn members; the skill measure runs ~8 points above the role-based measures from Stanford HAI and UNESCO (see Week 1 report).

At named major AI employers (US technical roles, 2024):
- **Google**: 27.2%
- **Microsoft**: 27.2% (+0.5 pp YoY)
- **NVIDIA** (most recent published): 19% total workforce, 12% global senior management (2022).

OpenAI, Anthropic, DeepMind, Meta AI: **do not break out AI-research-role gender publicly** in a comparable standardized form. `[methodology gap]`

**EU ICT specialists**: 19.5% women (Eurostat 2024). Range: 13.0% (Czechia) to 27.6% (Estonia). The US and EU averages are within 2 points of one another on the role-based measure.

**Singapore**: ~35% women in AI engineering talent — the global leader on LinkedIn's measure (2023).

**India**: NASSCOM/Deloitte 2024 flag a *decline* in women's representation at the junior professional level, despite 96% of Indian women in tech reporting intent to build AI skills. No clean single-figure share is publicly disclosed.

## 2. What's the trend?

Two trends move in opposite directions, and the divergence is the story.

**Trend 1 — entry-level women's share of AI/tech roles:** broadly flat. Google, Microsoft, NVIDIA all sit between 19–27% on technical roles. Year-over-year movement is in the noise.

**Trend 2 — the "broken rung":** the McKinsey/LeanIn 10-year data series shows the entry-level-to-manager promotion ratio has *barely moved*. From 281 companies and 10M+ employees: for every 100 men hired or promoted into entry-level/manager roles, **79 women in 2018 → 81 women in 2024**. A 2-point improvement over six years.

**Trend 3 — sectoral movement matters:** by industry, women's share of AI engineering skills (LinkedIn / OECD.AI):
- Education: 27% → 29% (rising)
- Tech/Manufacturing/Professional services: 23–30% (stable to rising)
- Financial Services: **31% → 28%** (declining)

Financial services going *backwards* on women in AI is the most economically consequential single sectoral movement in the data. It matters because financial-services AI roles compensate among the highest in the field.

**Trend 4 — the AI-tool adoption gap (new in 2025):** McKinsey/LeanIn 2025 reports entry-level women are encouraged by their manager to use AI tools at 21% vs. men at 33%. Randstad/Deloitte synthesis: at entry/mid-level, **33% of women vs. 44% of men are adopting AI tools**. This is the *first time* the AI-adoption gap has been quantified at the entry-tier; whether it persists is the most consequential thing to watch in 2026 data.

## 3. What's missing?

1. **No public AI/ML-specific hiring funnel by gender.** LinkedIn Economic Graph and Indeed Hiring Lab publish *stock* figures (who currently holds AI roles); neither publishes a *flow* funnel (applied → interviewed → offered → accepted) split by gender for AI roles specifically.

2. **AI residency programs do not disclose demographics.** Google AI Residency, Meta AI Residency, OpenAI Residency, Anthropic Research Programs — all explicitly recruit underrepresented applicants, none publicly disclose acceptance rates, gender composition, or conversion-to-FT rates. `[methodology gap]`

3. **No primary entry-level AI hiring share by gender for China, Brazil, or any African country.** The data densely cover the US and EU, lightly cover India, and effectively do not exist for the rest of the world.

4. **No published 12-month retention rate specific to entry-level women in AI/ML.** The 50%-leave-by-age-35 AnitaB.org statistic is the most-cited; it is **all of tech, not AI specifically**, and the underlying methodology is not regularly republished. The Kapor Center Tech Leavers Study (2017, n≈2,000) found 37% of women left their tech job primarily due to unfair treatment, women of color experienced stereotyping at ~2× the rate of white/Asian men — but this is now eight years old.

5. **CS-degree → AI-role conversion rate by gender (1–2 year outlook)** is not published by NSF NCSES or NCES. The National Survey of College Graduates allows tabulation but no off-the-shelf statistic exists.

6. **AnitaB.org Top Companies for Women Technologists paused in 2024.** This was the only multi-year benchmarking series of entry-level technical women at named employers. The pause is itself a data event.

## 4. What works?

**Programs with documented entry-level outcomes:**

- **Code2040 Fellows** (Black and Latinx CS undergraduates → tech internships → full-time conversion). ~90% return-offer rate (twice the industry baseline of ~45–50%); 100% subsequently work in tech. Population is intersectional by design — Black women, Latina women specifically. Source: Code2040 program data (self-reported).

- **AI4ALL** (high-school → college pipeline). 88% of alumni pursuing CS/AI college coursework; 86% interested in AI careers; 50% obtained an internship (2021 cohort). Self-reported.

- **Google Prep Tech_Afro expanded to all women in 2024** — outcome data not yet disclosed.

**Largest measured intervention with company-disclosed numbers:**

- **AnitaB.org Top Companies for Women Technologists** (paused 2024): women's tech-hire share rose for seven consecutive years through 2023, and attrition reached near-parity with men at participating firms. The seven-year benchmark is the strongest measured signal that **continuous public benchmarking moves employer behavior**.

**Documented entry-level retention intervention:**

- **Kapor Center Tech Leavers Study** identified specific causes of women's exit (37% citing unfair treatment as primary cause); no follow-up evaluation has measured whether reforms in cited areas (hiring fairness, manager training, recourse pathways) reduce exit.

The pattern across the strongest programs is identical to the pipeline finding: **multi-year, structurally embedded, and benchmarked.** Single events — even high-profile ones — do not show up in entry-level outcome data.

## 5. Recommended actions

1. **Read** — McKinsey/LeanIn *Women in the Workplace 2025* (specifically the AI-encouragement section). The 21% vs. 33% figure is the single most important new data point in entry-level women-in-AI for 2025.

2. **Share** — The **AI-tool encouragement gap (21% vs. 33%)** in your own organization, paired with a question to your team: *"What is the gender split of who in our org has been told to use AI tools by their manager?"* The 12-point gap is the most actionable single statistic for anyone working in an AI-adopting org right now.

3. **Ask** — Of any company doing AI hiring: *"What is your published 12-month retention rate for entry-level technical women, vs. men?"* If no such number exists, that is the lever to push on — measured retention is the prerequisite for fixing it.

4. **Track** — **Code2040** is the strongest measured intersectional intervention with public numbers. Watch the annual fellows-outcomes release. If their return-offer rate or industry-share drops, it is signal about broader entry-level Black and Latina women's outcomes.

5. **Act** — If you manage anyone, **make encouragement to use AI tools an explicit conversation in 1:1s with women you manage**. The 12-point gap is most likely driven by the absence of that conversation, not by a refusal to have it.

## Methodology notes for this report

The 21% vs. 33% AI-encouragement statistic is from McKinsey/LeanIn's *Women in the Workplace 2025*, which surveyed ~9,000 employees across 120+ North American firms. The figure is North America–specific; do not generalize globally without that caveat. The methodology is well-documented in the WitW series and replicable year-over-year.

LinkedIn Economic Graph's "AI engineering skills" measure includes anyone listing relevant skills, regardless of whether their current role is in AI — it overstates the AI-employed female population.

Next-week candidate follow-up: the **pay gap** (Week 5) is the downstream measurable consequence of differential hiring funnels and promotion velocity at entry.
