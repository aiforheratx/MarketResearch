# Women & AI research: authorship of top-tier papers

*Week 9 report — 2026-07-06 — Rotation row 7*

## Executive summary

At the field's defining venues, women write roughly **one paper in ten**: only **10.6% of first authors and 9.7% of senior (last) authors** at ICLR 2020, and about **13.8% of AI authors** across arXiv — a share that runs at roughly *half* women's ~24% presence in CS PhD programs, then shrinks again from junior to senior authorship, and again in citations, where female-first-authored NLP papers are cited **34% less** than men's (Mohammad, ACL 2020). The direction is not improvement but stagnation: female first-authorship in NLP has been flat since 2006 and AI co-authorship flat since the 1990s, even as entry-level pipelines widen. The single highest-leverage move a program chair or editor can make is to **adopt double-blind review** — the one intervention with causal evidence behind it that raising author anonymity raises women's acceptance and first-authorship.

## 1. What's the number?

Authorship is the one measure of standing in AI research that can be counted directly — the byline is public, and the last-author slot names who ran the lab. Counted that way, women are scarce, and scarcer the closer you get to the senior position.

**Top-venue authorship.** The most rigorous venue-level study opened the review records of a top ML conference: at **ICLR 2020**, only **10.6% of accepted papers had a female first author and 9.7% a female last author** — rising only to 12.3% and 13.4% among top-10-school submissions (Tran et al., *An Open Review of OpenReview*, 2020 — `[dated]`). A large-scale bibliometric baseline agrees on the order of magnitude: across roughly **1.5 million arXiv papers**, just **13.8% of AI-research authors were women** (Nesta, 2019 — `[dated]`). Industry counting from the same era landed lower still: Element AI's *Global AI Talent Report* found **~12% of authors** at the top 21 AI conferences were women, and only **18% of accepted papers had at least one female author** (2019, Tier 3 — `[dated]`).

**The senior-author gap.** Authorship position matters because in this field the last author is the principal investigator. In the ACL Anthology — the most complete corpus for a single AI subfield, NLP — women were **29.2% of first authors but 25.5% of last authors** across 1965–2019 (Mohammad, ACL 2020). The drop from ~29% to ~25% is the leaky pipeline made legible inside a single dataset: women are better represented as the junior, first-listed author than as the senior scientist who directs the work.

**The denominator that makes it stark.** These shares are not merely low; they are low *relative to the pool of women already credentialed to publish*. In 2019, women were **23.2% of US CS PhD students and 22.6% of CS faculty**, yet only **10.6%** of ICLR 2020 first authorships (Tran et al.). Top-venue authorship ran at roughly **half** the field's PhD-population share. Today that pool is larger — **24.1% of 2022–23 CS/CE/Information PhD recipients were women** (CRA *2023 Taulbee Survey*), rising to **25.9% in 2023–24** (CRA *2024 Taulbee*) — which only sharpens the question of where those women are in the author lists at NeurIPS, ICML, ICLR and CVPR.

## 2. What's the trend?

The defining feature of this topic is not the level but the *flatness*.

| Year | Statistic | Source |
|---|---|---|
| 1990s | AI co-authorship with ≥1 woman: no relative improvement since | Nesta 2019 |
| 2006–19 | NLP female first-authorship flat (~29%) for ~13 years | Mohammad 2020 |
| 2011 | 18.1% female AI PhD graduates (N. America) | Stanford HAI AI Index |
| 2021 | 21.3% female AI PhD graduates; <19% decade average | Stanford HAI AI Index |
| 2022 | 22.1% female CS PhD graduates — a slight *decrease* that year | Stanford HAI AI Index 2024 |
| 2023–24 | 25.9% female CS/CE/I PhD recipients — 8th straight annual rise | CRA Taulbee 2024 |

The trend is **improvement at the entry, stagnation at the byline**. Female first-authorship in NLP has "not improved since 2006" (Mohammad, ACL 2020), and the share of AI papers with a female co-author had not improved since the 1990s (Nesta). The pipeline that feeds authorship is widening slowly at the doctoral level — the CS PhD share has risen for eight consecutive years — but that entry-level gain has not yet propagated into the author lists of the flagship conferences, and the AI-specific PhD share (21.3% in 2021) has been essentially flat for a decade. Even the talent pool shows fragility: women's share of AI-engineering skill-listers **fell** for the first time to **29.4% in 2025** (WEF *Global Gender Gap Report 2025*). The gap does not close on its own.

## 3. What's missing?

The evidence base for this topic is, itself, a finding — and it is thinning.

- **No venue-specific women's-authorship share exists for 2023–2025.** The strongest hard numbers (ICLR 2020, ACL 1965–2019, Nesta's arXiv census) all predate 2021. There is no published, methodologically clean figure for women's share of NeurIPS, ICML, ICLR, CVPR or AAAI authorship in the last three years — a striking blind spot for a field that measures everything else about itself. Every top-line number in Section 1 is therefore flagged `[dated]`.
- **Stanford's AI Index has quietly dropped authorship-gender reporting.** The 2024 edition still carried CRA-based diversity figures (through 2022); the 2025 and 2026 editions surface **no** author-gender statistic. The single most-cited annual index of the field stopped counting.
- **No source cross-tabulates gender by AI specialty.** Taulbee reports the overall CS PhD gender share (~24–26%) and separately notes AI/ML is ~25% of doctorates, but never crosses the two — so the widely repeated "~19–21% of AI PhDs are women" figure cannot be verified against the primary survey. Treat it as `[methodology gap]`.
- **Gender inference is the method of record, and it is lossy.** Every study here infers gender from names, which mishandles unisex and many East-Asian names and erases non-binary researchers — a systematic bias baked into the entire literature.
- **The interventions that get promoted are the least measured.** WiML and Rising Stars publish attendance and placement counts, but no peer-reviewed study isolates their causal effect on submissions, acceptances, or authorship (see below).

## 4. What works?

One lever in this topic has genuine causal evidence behind it — and it is unusually cheap to pull.

- **Double-blind review.** The controlled experiment at **WSDM 2017** assigned every paper both single-blind and double-blind reviewers. Single-blind reviewers were markedly more generous to papers from famous authors (acceptance odds **×1.63**), top universities (**×1.58**) and top companies (**×2.10**); the female-author odds multiplier was **0.78**, not significant in that study alone but significant when pooled with prior experiments (Tomkins, Zhang & Heavlin, *PNAS* 2017 — `[dated]`). Because women are underrepresented among exactly the "famous/top-institution" authors that single-blind review rewards, removing names removes a headwind that lands disproportionately on them.
- **Journal-level evidence corroborates it.** When *Behavioral Ecology* switched to double-blind review in 2001, female first-authorship rose significantly while a comparable name-visible journal showed no such change (Budden et al., *Trends in Ecology & Evolution* 2008 — `[dated]`; the widely cited magnitude is ~7.9 points / ~33% relative, though the primary abstract states only "significant increase"). A parallel result appeared at the CS-adjacent **EvoLang** conference, where female first-authored papers were accepted at higher likelihood only after the move to double-blind review (Roberts & Verhoef, 2016 — `[dated]`).
- **Community infrastructure — strong participation, unproven causal effect.** The **Women in Machine Learning (WiML)** workshop, co-located with NeurIPS, drew a cohort that was **84.2% female-identifying** in 2022 and mobilizes 50+ senior scientists as mentors (Stanford HAI AI Index 2024, citing WiML). **Rising Stars in EECS** has run academic-career workshops for women since 2012, and MIT reports recent EECS hiring cohorts "nearly 50% women." Both are valuable, but their evidence is descriptive: no controlled evaluation ties attendance to later authorship.

*Untested but frequently cited:* fellowship programs for women researchers (DeepMind/Google/Meta), keynote-diversity pledges, and mentorship matching — widely promoted, none with a published comparison-group effect on authorship.

## 5. Recommended actions

1. **Read** — Tran et al., *An Open Review of OpenReview: A Critical Analysis of the ML Conference Review Process* (2020). It is the clearest venue-level dissection of how gender, seniority and institution move through the acceptance funnel — and the template for the fresh study the field is missing.
2. **Share** — the pairing of two numbers: women are ~24% of CS PhDs but only ~10% of top-venue first authors. The gap between the credential and the byline is the story, and it is roughly a factor of two.
3. **Ask** — of any conference or journal you influence: *"Are we double-blind, and if not, what is our defense given the PNAS evidence?"* It converts the strongest finding in this report into a governance question.
4. **Track** — the **female senior (last) author share** at the venue closest to your work. At ~9.7% (ICLR 2020) it is the lowest and most senior signal, the leading indicator of who will run AI labs in a decade — and, because the AI Index stopped reporting it, a number worth counting yourself.
5. **Act** — if you review, sponsor a first-time female first author onto a submission this cycle; if you chair or edit, move one venue to double-blind. It is the one change in this report with causal evidence that it shifts the authorship numbers.
