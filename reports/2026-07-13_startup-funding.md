# Women founders in AI startups: funding share

*Week 10 report — 2026-07-13 — Rotation row 8*

## Executive summary

The headline reads like a breakthrough — startups with at least one female founder captured a record **27.7% of all US venture deal value in 2025**, raising **$73.6 billion**, the first time women-founded companies cleared a quarter of the market — but the number is an artifact of two deals. **Two-thirds of every venture dollar that reached a female-founded startup last year flowed into AI**, and **Anthropic and Scale AI alone absorbed more than $30 billion of it**; strip those two out and female founders raised **$42.8 billion, less than they did in 2021 or 2022**, while companies founded *entirely* by women took just **1% of US VC in 2024** and deal counts fell for a fourth straight year. The single most important thing to understand this week is that the AI capital boom has not broadened access for women founders — it has concentrated it into a handful of mega-rounds that happen to list a woman among their co-founders, and the right number to watch is not the record top-line but the **all-women-team share**, still stuck at 1–2%.

## 1. What's the number?

The story of women's share of AI startup funding in 2025 is the story of a single statistic that means the opposite of what it appears to mean.

**The record that isn't.** On the top line, 2025 was the best year on record for female founders: US startups with at least one female founder raised **$73.6 billion and captured 27.7% of total US venture deal value** — clearing one-quarter of the market for the first time (PitchBook *2025 US All In*, via Fortune). Read alone, that looks like a decade of advocacy finally paying off. It is not. **Two-thirds of those dollars flowed into a single sector — AI — and nearly half of the AI money went to just two companies:** Anthropic (co-founded by Daniela Amodei; valued at $183B) and Scale AI (co-founded by Lucy Guo; valued at $74.1B), which between them absorbed **more than $30 billion**, over 40% of the AI total. **Set those two aside and female-founded teams raised $42.8 billion — less than they raised in 2021 or in 2022** (Fortune / PitchBook). The record is a composition effect: a handful of the largest rounds in venture history happen to list a woman among their co-founders, and they drag the aggregate share upward while the underlying market for women-led companies shrinks.

**The number that actually measures access.** The cleaner gauge of whether women can raise capital is the share going to **all-female founding teams** — companies where every founder is a woman, with no male co-founder to anchor the round. That number is brutal and barely moving: **1% of all US VC funding in 2024, across 6% of deals** — the lowest dollar share since 2017, down from 2% in 2023 (PitchBook *2024 US All In*). Even the broader "at least one female founder" cut, which flatters the picture, slipped in 2024: **deal-value share fell to 19.9% from 20.8%, and deal-count share to 25.1% from 26.4%**, even as the dollar figure rose to $38.8B (+27%) — because capital concentrated into fewer, larger rounds. Globally the floor is lower still: of **$289 billion** deployed in 2024, all-women teams received **2.3% ($6.7B)** against 83.6% for all-male teams (PitchBook).

The working figure this report treats as the honest headline is therefore **1–2%, not 27.7%.** The high number measures the gender of a co-founder on two trophy AI rounds; the low number measures whether a woman with an idea can get a check.

## 2. What's the trend?

The direction is concentration, not broadening — and by one key measure, regression.

| Year | Statistic | Source |
|---|---|---|
| 2021 | Peak year: dozens of female-founded US companies reached unicorn status | Crunchbase |
| 2023 | All-female teams: **2%** of US VC; >50% of US AI dollars touched a female-founded firm (skewed by OpenAI/Anthropic) | PitchBook; Crunchbase |
| 2024 | All-female teams: **1%** of US VC (6% of deals); ≥1-female-founder value share **19.9%** | PitchBook *2024 US All In* |
| 2025 | ≥1-female-founder value share hits record **27.7% ($73.6B)** — but two-thirds is AI, concentrated in two firms | PitchBook *2025 US All In* / Fortune |

Two things move in opposite directions. The **AI-inflated aggregate** rose sharply (from a $44.7B / ~20%-share base in 2023 to $73.6B / 27.7% in 2025), while the **access measures fell**: the all-female-team share halved from 2% to 1%, and **deal counts for female-founded companies declined for a fourth consecutive year**, having peaked in 2021 (Fortune / PitchBook). The trend is best described in one sentence: **the mega-round era is lifting the average while thinning the middle.** The women who founded or co-founded the frontier-model and data-labeling giants are raising sums that reset records; the far larger population of women starting AI companies below the mega-round tier is seeing fewer deals and a smaller slice. Europe shows the same stall from a lower base — female founders there raised **€5.76B (12% of VC) in 2024, down 12% in absolute terms**, with all-women teams stuck at **6% and flat since 2016** (Atomico *State of European Tech*).

## 3. What's missing?

The evidence base is unusually good on *dollars* and unusually poor on *AI-specific gender*, which is exactly the cut this topic needs.

- **No clean, recurring "female-founded share of *AI* funding" series exists.** The strong numbers — PitchBook's All In, Atomico's State of European Tech — measure *all* female-founded funding and then note, in passing, how much is AI. The AI-specific gender cut surfaces only in one-off analyses (Crunchbase's 2023 read that ">50% of AI dollars but <20% of AI rounds" touched a female founder). No source publishes, quarter over quarter, the share of AI-sector VC going to female founders, so the field cannot yet say whether the AI boom is widening or narrowing the gap — only that it is concentrating it.
- **"Has a female founder" hides who holds power.** The metric that moves the headline counts any team with at least one woman among the founders. It does not distinguish a solo female founder from a woman who is one of five co-founders, and it says nothing about ownership stake, board control, or CEO title. Anthropic and Scale AI both count toward "female-founded" in the 27.7% figure; neither is women-controlled. `[methodology gap]`
- **Seed and pre-seed AI, where women are most present, is the least measured.** Crunchbase maintains a "generative-AI, female-founded, seed-funded" list, but there is no audited denominator — no reliable count of how many AI startups have female founders at formation, against which to measure funding conversion.
- **Sector and health-AI granularity is thin.** For a medical-technology readership, the unanswered question is women's funding share in *health-AI and femtech specifically* — a cut no primary VC dataset breaks out cleanly (last flagged in the Week 8 healthcare-R&D report, where "women+ health" was 6.6% of US digital-health funding per Rock Health).

## 4. What works?

A few levers in venture funding have measured, not merely asserted, effects on how much capital reaches women.

- **Women writing the checks changes who gets funded.** The mechanism behind the funding gap is partly on the investor side of the table: firms with female partners are markedly more likely to fund female founders, and the investor base is overwhelmingly male. Building female decision-makers into partnerships — the explicit goal of programs like **All Raise** — is the intervention with the clearest line to the outcome, because it targets the allocator, not just the applicant. (All Raise's own tracking ties partner-level representation to female-founder deal share; treat magnitudes as advocacy-sourced, the direction as well-evidenced.)
- **Structured, blind-ish screening narrows the gap at the top of the funnel.** The venture analogue of double-blind review — evaluating traction and metrics before founder identity, and standardizing the diligence rubric — reduces the "pattern-matching" that penalizes founders who don't resemble prior winners. This echoes the one causally supported lever from last week's authorship report (double-blind review): **remove the identity signal early, and the numbers move.**
- **Dedicated capital pools convert to deals.** Funds with an explicit female-founder mandate (e.g., **Female Founders Fund**, and the growing set of European women-led funds tracked by European Women in VC) demonstrably deploy into companies the generalist market overlooks — a floor under the 1–2% rather than a fix for it.

*Untested but frequently cited:* pitch-competition prize money, demo-day diversity quotas, and one-off "women in AI" accelerator cohorts — visible and well-intentioned, but none with a published comparison-group effect on subsequent fundraising.

## 5. Recommended actions

1. **Read** — PitchBook's *2025 US All In: Female Founders in the VC Ecosystem* (and Fortune's 6 March 2026 analysis of it). Read it specifically for the Anthropic/Scale-AI adjustment: it is the clearest worked example of how a mega-round era can manufacture a record while access regresses.
2. **Share** — the two-number pairing that is the whole story: **27.7% of US venture value went to female-founded startups in 2025 — but strip out two AI companies and it's $42.8B, below 2021.** It inoculates any audience against the celebratory headline.
3. **Ask** — of any fund, corporate-venture arm, or investment committee you influence: *"What is our share of dollars — and of deals — going to all-women founding teams, not just teams with one female co-founder?"* The deal-count share is the honest number; demand it.
4. **Track** — the **all-female-team share of US VC** (1% in 2024). It is the leading indicator of genuine access, it is published annually by PitchBook, and unlike the AI-inflated aggregate it cannot be moved by a single trophy round.
5. **Act** — if you allocate capital or advise those who do, route one diligence process this quarter through identity-blind screening of metrics before founder bios; if you are building in health-AI, back or introduce one female-founded company in your sector, where the funding data is thinnest and the readership's leverage is highest.

The defining hazard in this topic is the **composition effect**: the "female-founded" label attaches to any team with at least one woman among the founders, so two 2025 mega-rounds — Anthropic and Scale AI — swing the aggregate US share to a record 27.7% while the access measures (all-female-team share, deal counts) regress. This report therefore treats the **1–2% all-female-team share** as the honest headline and flags the 27.7% figure as `[secondary]` and composition-driven. The largest genuine gap — noted in Section 3 — is the absence of any recurring, AI-specific *and* gender-specific funding series, and specifically the health-AI/femtech cut most relevant to this readership; that is the number to chase next.
