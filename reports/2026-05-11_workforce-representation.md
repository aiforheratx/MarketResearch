# State of women in AI: representation across the workforce

*Week 1 report — 2026-05-11*

## Executive summary

Women make up roughly **22% of the global AI workforce** (Stanford HAI AI Index 2024; UNESCO 2024) — a number that has barely moved in a decade despite ten years of public commitments. The story is not flat, though: women's share of **AI-skill-listing** professionals on LinkedIn rose from ~22% (2016) to **30.5% (2024)**, even as their share of *actual* AI roles stays stuck near 22% — the field is teaching more women than it is hiring or promoting. The single number worth carrying into every conversation about women in AI is the **leadership gap: under 14% of senior executive AI roles are held by women** (Interface EU 2024), and that is where action will pay the largest dividend.

## 1. What's the number?

The headline that most reports converge on is **22%**: roughly one in five people working in AI globally is a woman. Three independent sources triangulate to this figure for 2024:

- **Stanford HAI AI Index 2024** (Chapter 8: Diversity) reports 22% women across global AI roles, aggregating industry and academic data with "AI" defined broadly as AI-skilled workers and researchers.
- **Interface EU's 2024 study "AI's Missing Link"** analyzed approximately 1.6 million identifiable AI professionals worldwide and arrived at the same 22% figure. Methodology: gender predicted from first names matched to census data; binary categories only.
- **UNESCO's October 2024 "Women for Ethical AI" Outlook Study** decomposes the picture further: women are 20% of technical roles at major ML companies, 12% of AI researchers specifically, and 18% of AI startup executives.

These are **role-based** measures. They count people holding AI jobs.

A different number circulates in headlines: **30.5%**. This is the **LinkedIn Economic Graph** figure for women's share of members who *list AI engineering skills on their profile* (2024, via WEF Global Gender Gap Report 2024; 75-country sample). It is a **skill-supply** measure, not a role measure.

These two numbers are not in conflict — they are measuring different things. The supply of women with AI skills (≈30%) exceeds the share of women holding AI roles (≈22%) by 8–10 percentage points. The gap between **skill** and **role** is itself the most actionable finding in the data: the field has more qualified women than it employs. `[contested]` only if a source treats them as interchangeable.

Applying the 22% role figure to Interface EU's 1.6M sample implies roughly **352,000 women globally** in identified AI roles. No primary statistical body publishes a definitive absolute total — the global AI workforce itself is undercounted.

## 2. What's the trend?

Two different time-series, two different stories.

**Women's share of new AI PhDs (North America):**

| Year | % women | Source |
|---|---|---|
| 2011 | 18.1% | Stanford HAI AI Index 2023 |
| 2021 | 21.3% | Stanford HAI AI Index 2023 |
| 2022 | 22.9% (CS PhDs, AI-specialty proxy) | CRA Taulbee Survey 2023 |
| 2023 | 24.1% | CRA Taulbee Survey 2024 |
| 2024 | 25.9% | CRA Taulbee Survey 2024 (preliminary) |

Direction: **slowly improving** at roughly 0.5–1 percentage point per year. At this rate, parity in AI PhDs arrives around 2050.

**Women's share of AI engineering skill-listers (LinkedIn, global):**

| Year | % women |
|---|---|
| 2016 | ~22% |
| 2018 | 23.5% |
| 2024 | 30.5% |
| 2025 | 29.4% |

Direction: **rising for nearly a decade, plateaued in 2025**. Nesta has flagged that Europe's share specifically *declined* year-over-year (Germany's gap widened 4 pp).

**Women in AI roles (the role-based measure):** essentially flat at ~22% over the same window. The supply rose; the role share did not.

The summary trend: **pipeline is improving, hiring is not.** A field's representation cannot rise faster than the rate at which it actually hires and promotes the available people.

## 3. What's missing?

Six evidence gaps the data simply does not answer today:

1. **No primary source publishes women's share of Principal / Staff / Distinguished AI engineer roles industry-wide.** The closest proxy is Russell Reynolds' "22% across product/engineering/science leadership" cut across 39 AI organizations. AI Now Institute (2019) reported 15% of AI research staff at Facebook and 10% at Google were women — the only senior-IC benchmarks from named primary sources, and now seven years old.

2. **No 2023+ figure for Black women's share of AI technical roles.** The most-cited statistic — 3% of US computing — is from 2017 NCWIT and is *not* AI-specific. Women of color are demonstrably **31% of workers in the 15 most AI-vulnerable US jobs** (National Partnership 2024), so the absence of measurement at the top of the AI workforce is conspicuous.

3. **No global absolute headcount of women in AI** from any primary statistical body. Inferred totals (≈352,000 from Interface EU) are derivative.

4. **AI startup founder and funding gender data are fragmented.** UNESCO cites 0.7% of AI VC capital going to female-led startups; WEF cites a 31.7% EU-wide gap. These are not the same number, do not measure the same thing, and no consistent global series exists.

5. **Mid-career attrition from AI roles by gender is not tracked** by NSF NCSES, BLS, or Eurostat. We can count who enters; we cannot count who leaves.

6. **No cross-cut by AI sub-discipline.** Women's share of generative AI vs. classical ML vs. AI safety vs. AI infrastructure is absent from every major source. Anecdotally women cluster in NLP and AI ethics; the data does not yet confirm this.

## 4. What works?

Programs with **measured outcomes** (not just stated intentions):

- **Girls Who Code Summer Immersion Program.** Independent evaluation by the American Institutes for Research: participants were **13.2 percentage points more likely to major in a CS-related field** than a propensity-weighted comparison group; Self-Paced Program participants 11.5 pp more likely. Girls Who Code's own data shows alumni majoring in CS at **15× the national average** (Black/Latina alumni 16×). This is the strongest measured-outcome intervention in the female-pipeline literature.

- **Salesforce equal-pay audits.** Cumulative ~$10.3M spent on adjustments by 2019; $5.6M in 2022 alone. As of 2024, Salesforce now publishes adjusted pay ratios by gender and race in its disclosures. Demonstrates that **continuous, annualized auditing** moves the number — one-time audits do not.

- **AnitaB.org Top Companies for Women Technologists benchmark**: women's technical-hire share rose for seven consecutive years through 2023, and attrition reached near-parity with men at participating firms. **The program paused in 2024**, interrupting the only multi-year industry benchmark.

Cited but unmeasured (insufficient evidence to claim effectiveness): the 91% of organizations Deloitte says now "actively promote women in tech" (up from 76% in 2019). "Promoting" is not measured; outcomes are.

## 5. Recommended actions

1. **Read** — UNESCO's *Women for Ethical AI Outlook Study* (October 2024). It is the single most carefully methodologically-grounded global view in the literature today, and it disaggregates technical vs. research vs. executive in a way that makes downstream conversations sharper.

2. **Share** — The **gap between 30.5% AI skills and 22% AI roles** (LinkedIn vs. UNESCO/Stanford). It is the most economical way to puncture the "we can't find women" hiring argument: the women exist; the field is not hiring them in proportion to their supply.

3. **Ask** — In your org or community, ask: *"What is our director-and-above gender ratio in AI specifically, and what was it last year?"* If the answer is "we don't track AI separately," that is the answer.

4. **Track** — The **CRA Taulbee Survey** annual release (typically May). It is the only Tier 1 longitudinal source for the AI-PhD pipeline and the one that will tell you whether the pipeline-to-hiring ratio is improving.

5. **Act** — Make a written commitment in your next hiring loop to **proactively source from at least one of the named pipeline organizations** (WiML, Black in AI, LatinX in AI, AnitaB.org Top Companies alumni network). The 22% number does not move by accident.

## Methodology notes for this report

The LinkedIn 30.5% figure measures *self-listed AI engineering skills* on member profiles, not actual employment in AI roles. Stanford HAI and UNESCO 22% measure *people holding AI roles*. Treating these as comparable is the single most common error in popular coverage of women in AI; this report keeps them distinct.

Three Tier 1 PDFs (two Stanford HAI chapters, one LinkedIn PDF) returned binary content when retrieved during research and were verified via cross-referenced summaries. Spot-check the exact figures against the source PDFs before quoting them outside the brain.
