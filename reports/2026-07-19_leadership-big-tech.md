# Women in AI leadership at large tech companies

*Week 11 report — 2026-07-19 — Rotation row 9*

## Executive summary

Women hold **less than 14% of senior executive roles in AI** — and at the 39 leading AI organizations, just **10% of CEO and top-tech roles** — roughly half the 26–33% "women in leadership" figures large tech companies report about themselves, because women's leadership clusters in HR and legal (86% and 55% of those executive roles) rather than product, engineering, and science (22%). The trend broke in 2022: women's share of new C-suite hires plateaued at ~27% after seven years of steady gains, and in 2025 the S&P 500's female board share fell for the first time in a decade while **Google, Meta, and Microsoft stopped publishing diversity reports altogether**, ending the only decade-long public time series on who leads big tech. The single most important thing to understand this week is that the AI era's leadership cohort is being assembled right now inside companies that have stopped counting who gets a seat — the number to demand, and to watch, is the AI-and-technical-leadership cut, not the all-functions headline.

## 1. What's the number?

There are two sets of numbers for women's leadership in tech, and the gap between them is the finding.

**The numbers companies publish about themselves.** On self-reported "women in leadership" — each company defining leadership its own way — the large tech firms look like slow, steady progress: **Apple 33%** of leadership ("managers at all levels," global, 2024), **Google 32.8%** (global leadership, 2024), **Microsoft 29.0%** at the Executive level (global, 2024), **Amazon 26.3%** among senior leaders (level L8+, global, 2024), **Intel 19.0%** in senior leadership (salary grade 10+, 2023), and **Nvidia 13.5%** among "Leaders" (FY2025, ending January 2025). Meta's last published figure was **36.7% of director-level-and-above, from 2022** — the most flattering number in the set, and also the oldest, because Meta has published nothing since [dated]. These figures use incompatible denominators — Apple counts every manager, Amazon counts directors and VPs, Nvidia separates a small "Leaders" band — so they cannot be averaged, only ranged: roughly **one in four to one in three of big tech's self-defined leaders is a woman** `[methodology gap]`.

**The numbers measured about AI specifically.** When independent datasets isolate AI and technical leadership, the range halves. Interface's analysis of ~1.6M AI professionals in Revelio Labs data puts women at **22% of the AI workforce but less than 14% of AI senior executives** (2024) — a drop-off from ~29% at entry level. Russell Reynolds' census of 426 executives at 39 leading AI platform and application companies found women hold **30% of leadership roles overall — but 10% of CEO and top-tech roles** (four women CEOs, four women CTOs across all 39 firms), with four all-male leadership teams and nineteen firms below 25% women. The explanation for the 30%-vs-10% split is functional clustering: women are **86% of HR executives and 55% of legal executives at these AI firms, but 22% of product, engineering, and science executives** — the roles that decide what gets built. WEF/LinkedIn data says the same thing two ways: women hold **12.2% of STEM C-suite roles** against 24.4% of STEM managerial roles (2024), and globally are **8.6% of CTOs** — the lowest share of all 17 C-suite functions tracked, in the one function that owns AI (2025).

The working figures this report adopts: **~10–14% for AI and technical executive leadership** (Interface; Russell Reynolds; WEF CTO cut), against the **26–33%** all-functions headline the companies themselves report. Both are real; only the first one answers this week's question.

## 2. What's the trend?

Three phases: a decade of slow gains, a stall after 2022, and — since early 2025 — a measurement blackout.

| Year | Statistic | Source |
|---|---|---|
| 2015 | Women 17% of C-suite (US/Canada); 20.2% of new C-suite hires globally | McKinsey/LeanIn WitW; WEF/LinkedIn |
| 2020 | Google leadership 26.7% women; Amazon senior leaders 22.1% | Google DAR; Amazon workforce data |
| 2022 | New-C-suite-hire share peaks in growth at 26.6%; Meta files its final report (36.7%) | WEF/LinkedIn; Meta |
| 2024 | Google 32.8%; Amazon 26.3%; Microsoft executive level *falls* 0.1 pp — its only declining level; C-suite-hire share flat at ~27% | Company reports; WEF/LinkedIn |
| 2025 | Google/Meta/Microsoft publish nothing; S&P 500 women's board share falls 1 pp (first decline); US tech boards add the fewest new women directors in years (34% of new seats, down from 39%) | HR Brew/Allwork; Altrata; Spencer Stuart |

The direction in one sentence: **improving until 2022, stalled since, and now increasingly unmeasurable.** The WEF/LinkedIn hiring series is the cleanest evidence of the stall — women's share of new C-suite hires rose steadily from 20.2% (2015) to 26.6% (2022), then flatlined near 27% through 2025, with annualized growth in women CEO hires slowing from 3.9% to 1.7% and new CTO hires stuck at ~9%. The stall is visible inside companies too: Amazon's share of women among all people managers has been flat-to-declining since 2021 (31.1% → 31.2%), and Microsoft's executive level was its only level to fall in 2024. Governance shows the same inflection: Equilar recorded a seven-year low in new board seats going to women in Q4 2024, and Altrata recorded the S&P 500's first year-over-year decline in women's board share in Q1 2025. The structural context is the DEI retrenchment: Meta ended its DEI programs in January 2025, Google eliminated its representation goals in February 2025 (abandoning a 2020 pledge to improve underrepresented leadership 30% by 2025), IBM unlinked executive pay from diversity targets in April 2025, and Revelio Labs counts **2,600+ DEI-titled jobs cut since early 2023 (~13% of the total)** — roles in which **women were 71% of incumbents**. McKinsey/LeanIn's 2025 survey quantifies the priority collapse: **54% of companies now call women's advancement a high priority, versus 90% calling D&I a high priority in 2021.**

## 3. What's missing?

This topic now has a unique problem: the evidence base is *shrinking*.

- **The primary time series just ended.** Google, Meta, and Microsoft — three of the four largest AI employers — published no diversity report in 2025. Google's 11-year leadership series (20.8% → 32.8%) stops precisely at the moment its trajectory would have tested the DEI rollback's effect; Meta's data already ends in 2022. The Conference Board finds S&P 500 disclosure of women-in-management data fell 16% in 2025 filings. Whatever happens to women's leadership share at these companies from 2025 onward, no public number will record it.
- **No frontier AI lab publishes any workforce diversity data.** OpenAI, Anthropic, and DeepMind — the companies assembling the AI era's most consequential leadership — have never released a diversity report (DeepMind is folded into now-discontinued Alphabet reporting). A widely circulated "OpenAI 2024 DEI report" claim could not be verified against any OpenAI property and should not be cited.
- **No gender split exists for the Chief AI Officer.** Spencer Stuart tracks CAIO adoption (11% of medium-to-large organizations) but publishes no gender breakdown; the only number in circulation (~22% women, from one staffing firm's own placements) has undisclosed sample size `[methodology gap]`. A brand-new C-suite role is being staffed at scale with no demographic measurement at all.
- **"Leadership" is defined seven different ways.** Apple counts all managers; Amazon counts L8+; Intel counts grade 10+; Nvidia separates "Leaders" from "Managers"; Microsoft reports a bespoke "Executive" band; LinkedIn-based studies infer seniority from titles. No two company numbers in Section 1 are directly comparable, and none isolates *AI* leadership within the company `[methodology gap]`.
- **The intersectional cut is absent.** No large tech company published race × gender leadership data for 2025, and none of the AI-specific studies (Interface, Russell Reynolds) disaggregates women of color in AI leadership.

## 4. What works?

The interventions with measured outcomes share one property: they change the promotion mechanism, not the messaging.

- **Sponsorship, measured, roughly doubles promotion odds.** In McKinsey/LeanIn's 2025 data, 65% of employees with a sponsor were promoted within the prior two years versus 35% without, and employees with multiple sponsors were more than twice as likely to advance — yet only 31% of entry-level women have a sponsor (vs 45% of men), and women are half as likely to have a *senior* sponsor (16% vs 33%). Coqual's earlier *Sponsor Dividend* work found that **when women have sponsors they are promoted at the same rate as men** — sponsorship closes the gap, mentorship does not.
- **Removing the self-nomination filter closed Google's promotion gap.** When Google found women engineers self-nominated for promotion at lower rates, it began sending data-backed nudge emails; women's application rates rose to parity or above [dated — 2014, company-reported]. The lesson survives the datedness: the gap lived in the process, not the pipeline.
- **Public, dated targets moved the number at Accenture.** After setting public 2017 goals, Accenture reached **48% women overall and 30% women managing directors by FY2024** — targets substantially met before the program was sunset in February 2025. It remains the clearest large-scale before/after for target-setting in tech services.
- **Compensation-linked goals moved it at Intel.** Intel tied manager pay to diversity goals under a $300M commitment and reached its US "full representation" target two years early, with women in leadership rising 17.7% (2015) → 20.7% (2018) [dated]; note Intel's definition benchmarks against skilled-labor-market availability, not parity.
- **A caveat that is itself a finding:** in 2025 data, mostly-remote women are markedly less likely to have a sponsor (37% vs 54% of on-site women) and less likely to be promoted — a flexibility stigma that hits women specifically. Flexibility aids retention but currently carries a measured promotion penalty.

*Untested but frequently cited:* Microsoft LEAP, Google #IamRemarkable, Salesforce Amplify, Deloitte's Women in Technology program, and most board-readiness programs (Athena Alliance, 50/50 Women on Boards workshops) — visible, plausible, and none with published before/after leadership outcomes.

## 5. Recommended actions

1. **Read** — Russell Reynolds' *AI for All: The Case for More Women Leaders in AI* (2025). It is the only executive-level census of actual AI companies, and its functional split (86% of HR executive roles vs 22% of technical executive roles) is the single most clarifying breakdown of why "30% of leadership" and "10% of top-tech roles" coexist.
2. **Share** — the pairing that inoculates against the corporate headline: **big tech reports 26–33% women in leadership, but women hold under 14% of senior AI executive roles, 10% of CEO/top-tech roles at leading AI firms, and 8.6% of CTO seats globally.**
3. **Ask** — of any leadership team or board you can reach, including in medtech: *"What is women's share of our technical and AI leadership specifically — not leadership overall — and will we keep publishing it now that our peers have stopped?"* The disclosure question is now as important as the representation question.
4. **Track** — the **WEF/LinkedIn share of new C-suite hires going to women** (~27%, plateaued since 2022) and the **new-CTO-hire share (~9%)**. With company reporting going dark, hiring-flow data from platform sources is becoming the only remaining public signal, and hires are a leading indicator where stock representation lags.
5. **Act** — if you run or influence a promotion cycle this quarter, implement the two mechanisms with measured effects: name a sponsor (not a mentor) for each high-potential woman in a technical track, and remove self-nomination as the gate for promotion candidacy. Both moved numbers at scale; neither requires a program budget.

## Methodology notes for this report

This is Week 11, standard rotation row 9 (women in AI leadership at large tech companies); the featured Healthcare R&D track last ran 2026-06-29 (within its 4-week window) and resumes next week with H2 (gender bias inside medical AI), when fresh evidence permits an override. The defining hazard in this topic is the **denominator problem**: "women in leadership" spans seven incompatible company definitions (all managers → L8+ → grade 10+ → a bespoke "Leaders" band), and none of them isolates AI or technical leadership — so this report treats the independent AI-specific measurements (Interface <14%; Russell Reynolds 10% top-tech; WEF 8.6% CTO) as the working figures and the 26–33% corporate headlines as context. The second hazard is **survivorship of disclosure**: from 2025 the companies still publishing (Amazon, Apple, Nvidia) are a self-selected subset, so any cross-company average will be biased by who still reports. WEF's 31.7% (GGGR 2024, "senior leadership") and 28.8% (GGGR 2025, "top-level management") reflect different LinkedIn seniority cuts, not a year-over-year decline; the two WEF figures used here (12.2% STEM C-suite; 8.6% CTO) come from separate 2025/2026 publications with stated definitions. UNESCO's widely repeated "12% of AI researchers" has a pre-2020 origin and was deliberately excluded from the headline set in favor of 2024–2026 measurements. The gap to chase next: any gender data at all from OpenAI, Anthropic, or DeepMind, and a CAIO gender census.
