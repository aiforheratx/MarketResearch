# Graduate school: women in AI/ML PhD programs

*Week 3 report — 2026-05-25*

## Executive summary

Women are **25.9% of 2023–24 CS/CE/I PhD recipients** in North America (CRA Taulbee 2024) — the **eighth consecutive annual rise**, the strongest sustained positive trend in any women-in-AI dataset. But the AI-specialty subset tells a sharper story: women have been **under 19% of AI PhD graduates across the past decade**, and the most-replicated finding in the academic literature — the **advisor-gender effect** — shows that female PhD students with female advisors persist longer, publish more, and place better. The single most consequential gap: **no public dataset isolates AI-PhD completion rates by gender**, so we know who enrolls and who graduates but cannot count who silently leaves.

## 1. What's the number?

**Enrolled CS PhD students who are female: 22.7%** (CRA Taulbee 2023 Survey — 182 PhD-conferring departments in the US and Canada; 77.2% male, 0.1% nonbinary).

**CS/CE/I PhD recipients who are female: 25.9% in 2023–24**, up from 24.1% in 2022–23 and 22.9% in 2021–22 (CRA Taulbee 2024; 157 of 314 departments responding, 50% response rate). This is the **eighth consecutive year of increase** in women's share of enrolled doctoral students (21.6% in 2015–16 → 27.1% in 2023–24).

For the AI-specialty subset specifically: **AI/ML is the single most popular PhD specialty** in CS (>25% of doctorates where specialty is known), and **women have been under 19% of AI PhD graduates** across the 2013–2022 decade (Stanford HAI AI Index 2024, using CRA Taulbee). Stanford HAI's annualized cut: **2021 AI PhD graduates were 21.3% female**, up from 18.1% in 2011 — a 3.2 percentage-point rise across a decade.

The asymmetry to register: **broader CS PhDs are at 25.9% women and rising; AI-specialty PhDs are at ~21% and rising slower.** The AI sub-field is *less* female than CS as a whole, not more.

## 2. What's the trend?

| Year | Female % of CS PhD recipients (CRA aggregate) |
|---|---|
| 2018–19 | 20.3% |
| 2020–21 | ~21.3% (AI-specific) |
| 2021–22 | 22.9% |
| 2022–23 | 24.1% (CS-only: 22.7%) |
| 2023–24 | 25.9% |

Direction: **rising at ~1.5 percentage points per year for three years**, the steepest stretch in the recorded series. The slow rise in AI-specific PhDs (~0.3 pp/year over the past decade) is below the CS-aggregate pace.

The faculty pipeline trend is **bifurcated**:
- Women's share of *new* CS/CE/I faculty hires: **24.9% (2017) → 30.2% (2021)** — improving.
- But: CRA Taulbee 2022–23 reported new tenure-track faculty hires fell from 25.9% women to 20.0% women in one year — **below the 22.9% female share of new PhDs**. One bad year, but it broke the pattern.
- Female faculty at top-18 universities whose primary research focus is AI: **16.1%** (Stanford HAI 2024).

The summary: **PhD inflow is the brightest line on the chart; faculty hiring is the noisiest; AI-specific representation lags the CS-aggregate measure across every layer.**

## 3. What's missing?

1. **No public dataset of AI-PhD completion rates by gender.** CRA Taulbee tracks enrollment and degree production separately but does not publish cohort-level persistence. We have enrollment counts and degree counts; we cannot compute "of women who started an AI PhD in 2018, what % had a degree by 2024?" This is the single largest gap in the women-in-AI evidence base.

2. **No gender-disaggregated AI postdoc population.** NSF GSS reports postdoc gender for all S&E but not AI as a subfield. The postdoc layer is where many academic women in AI silently leave the academic track.

3. **No intersectional race × gender breakdown for CS/AI PhDs.** CRA Taulbee 2023 reports CS PhDs awarded: Black/African-American 1.2% (18 recipients), Hispanic any race 2.1% (32 recipients), Asian 10.0%, White 19.7%, Nonresident alien 66.2%. Combined gender cuts require NSF SED restricted-access microdata.

4. **No measured longitudinal placement outcomes for WiML or Rising Stars in EECS.** Both interventions are widely used; neither has published comparison-group placement data.

5. **Stanford, MIT, CMU, Berkeley do not publish program-specific AI PhD gender breakdowns** in standardized form — each program's numbers exist; no public roll-up does.

6. **The 2024 Taulbee AI-specialty × gender cross-tab is not publicly summarized.** The last published one was in 2019 ("Expanding the Pipeline," women = 13.4% of AI/ML PhD recipients, 2012–2018 aggregate).

## 4. What works?

**Strongest measured-outcome intervention in the women-in-AI literature:**

- **CRA-W Grad Cohort for Women.** A masters-and-PhD-stage workshop bundling structured mentorship, technical-talk practice, and CV review. Outcomes published in PNAS (PMC5222789): **67% of master's-stage attendees intended to pursue a PhD vs. 29% of matched non-participants**; **71% first-author publication rate vs. 23%** in the 1–3 years post-workshop. This is matched-cohort survey evidence — not RCT but the cleanest available.

**Widely used, no rigorous published outcomes:**

- **Rising Stars in EECS** (MIT, annual since 2012). Late-stage PhDs and postdocs of underrepresented genders preparing for academic careers. Documented placements at top universities, but **no published longitudinal placement rate** vs. a comparison group.
- **WiML Workshop** (NeurIPS-colocated, since 2006). 2020 workshop attendance was 89.5% women / nonbinary, 10.4% men; mentorship program funds PhD application fees and pairs >50 senior ML mentors. **No completion or placement outcomes** in peer-reviewed form.

**The advisor effect** — separately, an evidence-rich finding the literature now treats as robust:

- **Canaan & Mouganie** (*Journal of Human Resources*) used quasi-experimental advisor assignment data and found female science advisors **substantially raise the likelihood that women enroll in and graduate with STEM degrees** and **reduce time-to-degree**. [paywalled]
- **Gaule & Piacentini** (*Research Policy*) found female PhD students with female advisors **publish more** than female students with male advisors. [paywalled]
- **Bostwick & Weinberg** (NBER / *Journal of Labor Economics*): in "typically male" doctoral programs (engineering, physics, CS-adjacent), women are **16 percentage points less likely to complete a PhD**; and **women entering a first-year cohort with no female peers are 10 pp more likely to drop out before year 2**. A 10-pp rise in female cohort share lifts on-time graduation by 1 pp.

The mechanism is consistent across the three: **structural exposure to other women — peers and advisors — measurably moves persistence and productivity.** This is the strongest case in the literature for *active* cohort-shaping and faculty-recruitment as interventions rather than passive recruitment.

## 5. Recommended actions

1. **Read** — The **CRA-W Grad Cohort evaluation** (PMC5222789). It is the cleanest comparison-group evidence for a graduate-stage women-in-CS intervention and the right citation for any conversation about whether structured mentorship "works."

2. **Share** — The **advisor-effect bundle** (Canaan & Mouganie; Gaule & Piacentini; Bostwick & Weinberg). Three independent designs converging on the same finding is unusual in this literature. It is the cleanest research-backed argument for **increasing female faculty representation in AI programs as a persistence intervention**, not just a representation one.

3. **Ask** — Of any AI PhD program in your network: *"What is your female-graduate enrollment %, and what % of your AI-specialty faculty are women?"* The ratio of those two numbers tells you whether the program is set up to retain the women it admits.

4. **Track** — The **annual CRA Taulbee release** (typically May). Watch the AI-specialty × gender cross-tab specifically — it was last published in 2019 and an update is overdue.

5. **Act** — If you mentor a woman in an AI PhD, **introduce her to at least one woman one career stage ahead of her** before the academic year ends. The peer-effect literature says cohort-shaping is measurably consequential; the smallest unit of cohort-shaping you can do is one introduction.

## Methodology notes for this report

CRA Taulbee covers US and Canadian PhD-granting departments only; it is the canonical longitudinal series for CS doctoral demographics in North America but does not directly capture industry AI labs (DeepMind, OpenAI, Anthropic) where many AI researchers now train without a PhD. The "women in AI" data anchored to CRA underestimates the modern industry-trained AI researcher population by an unknown amount.

The three advisor-effect papers are paywalled but all have accessible working-paper versions; cite the working papers if writing for an open-access audience.
