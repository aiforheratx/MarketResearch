# The pipeline: women in undergraduate CS and AI programs

*Week 2 report — 2026-05-18*

## Executive summary

Women earned **22.6% of US computer science bachelor's degrees in 2021–22** (NCES IPEDS) — *less than the historical peak of 37.1% in 1983–84*. The deepest finding in the trend data is that CS is one of the only STEM fields where women's share **went down for nearly thirty years before recovering** (trough of 17.6% in 2010–11). The single most actionable lever is **AP Computer Science Principles**, which carries a 34% female participation rate vs. AP CS A's 26% — the introductory course is where the pipeline either widens or narrows, and the data is unambiguous about which version works.

## 1. What's the number?

In the United States, **22.6% of computer and information sciences bachelor's degrees were awarded to women in 2021–22** (NCES Digest of Education Statistics, Table 325.35 — IPEDS data covering all Title IV institutions). This is the canonical figure cited by virtually every downstream report.

At PhD-granting CS departments specifically, **CRA Taulbee 2024 reports 23.7%** of CS bachelor's recipients are women. The two numbers (NCES national 22.6%; CRA at PhD-granting departments 23.7%) bracket the true national figure within roughly one point.

There is **no Tier 1 national statistic for women's share of AI-specific undergraduate concentrations**. IPEDS CIP code 11.0102 ("Artificial Intelligence") was added recently; institutional reporting is sparse. Stanford HAI's AI Index uses **CRA Taulbee AI-specialty PhD data as a proxy** — and on that proxy, women have been under 19% of AI-specialty PhDs across the past decade. The undergraduate AI-specific number is `[methodology gap]`: it does not exist yet.

International benchmarks:
- **UK**: 26% of computing entrants are women (HESA SB271, 2023–24).
- **EU-27**: ~23% of ICT tertiary graduates are women (OECD Education at a Glance 2023, drawing on Eurostat).
- **India**: 34.2% of CS Engineering undergraduate enrollment is women (AICTE/AISHE, 2021–22) — the highest of the major AI-producing economies.
- **China**: no openly published national CS undergrad gender figure. Single-institution data (Tsinghua) shows <10% (2013, dated). `[methodology gap]`

## 2. What's the trend?

The US trend is the story. Women's share of CS bachelor's degrees did not march upward over 50 years like most STEM fields. It **rose**, **collapsed**, and is **slowly recovering**.

| Year | % women, US CS BAs |
|---|---|
| 1970–71 | 13.6% |
| 1980–81 | 32.5% |
| **1983–84** | **37.1% (historical peak)** |
| 1984–85 | 36.8% |
| 1990–91 | 29.4% |
| 2000–01 | 27.7% |
| **2010–11** | **17.6% (trough)** |
| 2015–16 | 18.7% |
| 2018–19 | 20.7% |
| 2020–21 | 21.9% |
| 2021–22 | 22.6% |

Source for every cell: NCES IPEDS Digest Table 325.35.

The 1984–85 peak is one of the most-cited statistics in tech-gender literature because it falsifies a common framing: this is not a "computers are for boys" *natural* equilibrium. The PC's mass-market arrival, the rebranding of computing as a boys' hobby, the canonical "personal computer for the family son" advertising of the 1980s, and the simultaneous rise of intro-CS courses that assumed prior coding experience are widely cited explanations. Whichever mix of causes mattered most, the **trajectory is reversible** — the data shows it once was higher.

The AP pipeline shows where momentum is now:

| AP exam | 2023 total | Female | % female |
|---|---|---|---|
| AP Computer Science Principles | 164,505 | 55,572 | **33.8%** |
| AP Computer Science A | 96,588 | 24,147 | **25.0%** |
| AP CS A | 98,136 | 25,811 | **26.3%** (2024) |

AP CS A grew +7% YoY and +81% since 2017 in female participation. **AP CSP has held a ~9-point higher female share than AP CS A every year since CSP launched.**

## 3. What's missing?

1. **No standardized national data on women in AI-specific undergrad majors or concentrations** (only the PhD proxy). The IPEDS CIP for AI exists but institutional reporting is too thin to derive a Tier 1 number.

2. **No national US attrition rate for women in CS undergrad.** Single-institution figures circulate — Penn pre-NCWIT showed 13.6% (women) vs. 2.5% (men); a CACM-cited study found 21% vs. 15% annual attrition. CRA Taulbee explicitly does not measure persistence. We know the leaky middle exists; we cannot yet size it nationally.

3. **China CS undergrad gender data are not openly published** at the national level.

4. **Race-by-gender CS bachelor's counts** are derivable from IPEDS microdata but not published as a Tier 1 single number. Most cited "Black women in CS" or "Latina in CS" figures circulate via Tier 2 advocacy compilations.

5. **AI4ALL and Kode With Klossy lack independent third-party impact evaluations** comparable to the AIR evaluation of Girls Who Code. Self-reported alumni outcomes are not the same as comparison-group evidence.

## 4. What works?

**Programs with rigorous independent evaluation:**

- **Girls Who Code Summer Immersion Program.** The American Institutes for Research (AIR) ran a quasi-experimental evaluation (inverse-propensity weighting against waitlisted comparison; published March 2024, ERIC ED647331). Participants were **13.2 percentage points more likely to major in a CS-related field** than the comparison group; Self-Paced Program participants 11.5 pp more likely. Girls Who Code self-reports alumni at **15× national average** for CS majors; Black/Latina alumni at 16×. The AIR finding is the strongest measured-outcome result in the female-pipeline literature.

- **BRAID Initiative** (Building Recruiting and Inclusion for Diversity), UCLA-led longitudinal study under Linda Sax (NSF Award 1525737). Across 15 participating CS departments: **108% increase in women's computing enrollment** over the BRAID period; women's enrollment growth outpaced overall enrollment growth at all 15 institutions. The intervention bundle included intro-CS pedagogy redesign, dedicated student support, and explicit faculty engagement — not single-point activities.

**Programs with strong self-reported outcomes but no independent evaluation:**

- **Black Girls CODE.** Rockman et al. 2017 case study documented gains in coding self-efficacy and tech-career interest. No longitudinal college-major outcome data has been published.

- **AI4ALL.** 88% of alumni continuing CS/AI; 50% landed internships (2021 cohort) per self-report. No independent third-party evaluation published.

- **Kode With Klossy.** Frequently cited; no independent evaluation located in this research pass.

The structural lesson from BRAID and Girls Who Code: **the interventions that move the number are multi-year, departmental or curricular, and measured.** Single-day events, sponsorship logos, and "Day for Girls" promotions show up in zero published evaluations as moving the major-completion rate.

## 5. Recommended actions

1. **Read** — The **AIR Girls Who Code evaluation** (ERIC ED647331, March 2024). It is the cleanest comparison-group evidence in the female-pipeline literature and the right reference any time someone claims "we tried that program and it didn't work."

2. **Share** — The **1984 → 2010 trough chart** (37.1% → 17.6%). It is the most economical visual rebuttal to the "girls just don't like CS" argument. Whatever your audience's prior, the 30-point collapse forces them to update.

3. **Ask** — Of any high school in your network: *"Does this school offer AP CS Principles, and what is the female enrollment ratio?"* The answer doubles as a school-quality signal.

4. **Track** — The annual **NCES IPEDS Digest Table 325.35** release. It is the canonical longitudinal series for US CS-bachelor's gender share and the number to watch for whether the 2010s recovery continues.

5. **Act** — If you fund, mentor, or sponsor any pipeline program, **prioritize the ones with independent third-party evaluations** (Girls Who Code, BRAID-affiliated departments). The opportunity cost of funding an unmeasured program is funding a measured one.

## Methodology notes for this report

"Computer and information sciences" in IPEDS is broader than "computer science" alone; it includes information sciences, information systems, and related concentrations. The NCES 22.6% figure is the standard cited benchmark but is *not* CS-only. The CRA Taulbee 23.7% is CS-only and PhD-granting-institution-only — closer to the "elite CS" reference frame but smaller in coverage.

Race-by-gender intersection data are derivable from IPEDS microdata but were not extracted to a single national figure in this pass. A future report should commission this cut directly.
