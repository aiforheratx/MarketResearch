The pay gap in AI and machine learning roles

Cash gaps have narrowed. Equity gaps have not. In AI, where compensation concentrates in equity, the under-measured gap is the larger gap.

Filed: 2026-06-08 Length: ~2,200 words Sources cited: 22 Tier 1: 7 · Tier 2: 12 · Tier 3: 3
35% / 20%
Women are 35% of equity-holding tech employees but hold only 20% of equity value (Carta Gap Table). A 5-to-1 underweight that dwarfs the cash gap and is the most under-discussed statistic in the women-in-AI pay literature.
Executive summary
Women in US computer-and-mathematical occupations earn $0.80 per $1.00 of men's median weekly earnings (BLS 2024, uncontrolled). The more useful statistic is the controlled vs. uncontrolled split: for the Computer & Mathematical group Payscale 2024 reports $0.92 uncontrolled / $0.97 controlled. The single most underreported finding is from Carta's Gap Table: women are 35% of equity-holding tech employees but hold only 20% of equity value — a 5-to-1 underweight. Cash gaps are smaller and have narrowed; equity gaps remain unaddressed and are where lifetime AI compensation is actually decided.

The cash gap, the equity gap, and the intersectional cliff
Three lenses on the same compensation question. The equity gap is the largest. The intersectional gap is the most under-measured inside AI.
Sources: BLS Highlights of Women's Earnings 2024; Payscale 2024/2026 Gender Pay Gap Report (Computer & Math group); Carta Analyzing the Gender Equity Gap (Gap Table) — ~6,000 cap tables and ~$45B equity value; NWLC / IWPR National Wage Gap Fact Sheet 2024; Pew Research Center 2024.

What's the number?

The headline that hits most readers is $0.80 per $1.00 — women's median weekly earnings in US Computer & Mathematical occupations, full-time wage and salary workers (BLS Highlights of Women's Earnings 2024). This is uncontrolled: it does not adjust for role, level, experience, location, or hours.

The discrepancy between BLS (0.80) and Payscale (0.92) on the same group is methodological: BLS uses CPS median weekly earnings with no controls; Payscale uses a different sample with hours and structure normalization. Both numbers are correct for what they measure. The controlled $0.97 is the residual gap after observable adjustments — the part the field still cannot explain.

No primary source publishes a clean "AI engineer" or "data scientist" pay gap specifically. BLS does not yet have a separate SOC code for AI engineers; Payscale and Levels.fyi do not publish AI-only cuts. The headline figures here treat the broader Computer & Mathematical SOC group as the working proxy.

What's the trend?

By level (Payscale 2026, all industries): IC $0.83 uncontrolled / $1.00 controlled; Manager $0.83 / $0.95; Director $0.82 / $0.93; Executive $0.69 / $0.92. The controlled gap widens with seniority; the uncontrolled gap is roughly constant IC-through-Director, then drops sharply at the executive level. Levels.fyi Q1 2024 describes the same pattern qualitatively: the gap "gets more pronounced at higher levels in the US."

Direction over time: Pew Research finds the US overall gap has narrowed only slightly over 20 years. The Colorado pay-transparency law (effective Jan 2021) is the cleanest natural experiment — state gap narrowed from 78¢ (2021) to 86¢ (Jul 2025), a 7-point gain in three and a half years that does not show up at comparable speed in BLS national data.

What's missing?

  1. No primary source isolates the AI/ML pay gap. No SOC code, no Payscale/Levels.fyi cut.
  2. No intersectional pay data inside AI/ML roles. All available figures are all-occupation.
  3. No equity-equity audit at any AI lab. Given AI offers are 60–80% equity, this is the largest under-measured component of women's AI pay.
  4. Levels.fyi Q1 2024 shows charts but withholds exact %-gap numbers by level.
  5. No India-specific AI/ML gender pay number. NASSCOM 2024 covers AI/ML premiums but not by gender.
  6. No lifetime earnings projection specific to AI careers. NWLC 40-year cumulative figures ($430,480 avg; $877,480 Black women; $1,007,080 Latinas) are all-occupation.

What works?

  • Salesforce equal-pay audits (annual, since 2015). Cumulative ~$10.3M in adjustments by 2019; $5.6M in 2022 alone. As of 2024, Salesforce publishes adjusted pay ratios by gender and race. Continuous annualized auditing prevents new gaps from forming.
  • Colorado Equal Pay for Equal Work Act. State gap 78¢ (2021) → 86¢ (Jul 2025). Cullen (JEP 2024) finds posted salaries rose +3.6% on average; horizontal transparency can compress pay downward. Salary-info job postings rose ~30 pp post-law per Arnold et al.
  • Negotiation gap — newly reframed. Vanderbilt / Kennedy (2023) finds women now negotiate as often or more often than men. Pew (April 2023): most US workers — both sexes — do not negotiate starting pay. Most of the gap is offered, not asked.
Recommended actions
  1. Read — Carta's Gap Table report. Single best evidence base for the cash-vs-equity decomposition.
  2. Share — The 35% equity-holders / 20% equity value statistic. Most economical way to puncture "tech pay is roughly fair now."
  3. Ask — Of your employer or mentor: "What is our equity-equity audit, separately from our cash-pay audit?" If the answer is "we don't do one," that is the answer.
  4. Track — The annual BLS Highlights of Women's Earnings release. Computer & Mathematical occupational gap is the longest-running consistent series.
  5. Act — If you are negotiating, negotiate the equity component explicitly, not just base salary. The equity gap is wider than the cash gap and the one most likely to be silently agreed to.
