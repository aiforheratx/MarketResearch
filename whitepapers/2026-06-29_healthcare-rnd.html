Women & AI in healthcare R&D: the state of representation

Women are a minority at every layer of medical AI — and the gap is being encoded into the algorithms.

Filed: 2026-06-29 Length: ~2,400 words Sources cited: 17 Tier 1: 9 · Tier 2: 4 · Tier 3: 4

Executive summary
Women are a structural minority at every layer of AI in healthcare R&D: roughly 12% of AI researchers and 16% of AI academic faculty (UNESCO, 2024), and under 40% of authors on medical-AI papers — a share that, in radiology, falls the more central AI becomes to the work. This is not only an equity problem; it is encoded into the product, with state-of-the-art chest-X-ray AI shown to systematically underdiagnose female patients (Nature Medicine, 2021), built on trial data where women were only ~34–38% of participants (JACC, 2018; Circulation, 2020). The most useful move a medtech R&D leader can make this week is to audit one model's training data for sex balance — because the representation gap in the room is reproducing itself in the algorithm.

What's the number?

There is no single audited figure for "women's share of the health-AI workforce" — it is the intersection of two fields that each measure gender separately and neither measures cleanly. So the picture has to be assembled from four adjacent measurements, each with a named source. The research layer: UNESCO's 2024 Women for Ethical AI study puts women at 12% of AI researchers, 16% of AI faculty, and 18% of AI-startup executives, framing the research-position gap (38%) as nearly double the 21% gap in general science R&D. Stanford HAI corroborates the pipeline — women are ~22.1% of CS PhD graduates and 16% of tenure-track CS faculty (2024 report, data to 2022, dated) — and the 2026 AI Index calls the diversity needle still "unmoving." The authorship layer is measurable directly: in AI radiology research (453 articles, 24 countries) women were 22.3% of first and 27.3% of senior authors (Yan et al., CARJ 2023); in AI breast-imaging, 35.7% first and 37.4% senior (Eur J Radiology 2024). The capital layer: PitchBook's 2024 All In report finds all-female founding teams captured ~1–2% of US VC, with female-founder deal value slipping from 20.8% to 19.9%; Rock Health shows $671M (6.6%) of 2024 digital-health funding went to "women+ health." The leadership and engineering layer: in biopharma, ~20% of CSOs and ~14% of CTOs are women (Pharma's Almanac 2025); in medtech, women are ~40% of the workforce but 21% of executives (Med Design & Outsourcing 2022); at the bench, women are 27% of US business-R&D employees but just 12% in engineering/technology R&D (NSF 23-328, 2020 data).

Women are 34% of the cardiovascular trial participants whose data trains the model — and the AI is then deployed to both sexes. Under-representation in the room becomes under-representation in the data becomes under-diagnosis in the clinic.

What's the trend?

Where time series exist, movement is slow and uneven: AI-talent share crept from ~25% (2016) to ~27% (2024) (WEF GGGR, LinkedIn data); biotech executive teams climbed from ~25% (2018) to 35% (2021) and then plateaued (PharmaVoice); female senior-authorship in AI breast-imaging is rising but still below 40%. The pattern is slow gains at the entry/skill layer, a plateau at leadership, and stagnation in the underlying data. The ILO added a sharper warning in 2025: women face higher workplace exposure to generative-AI automation than men — so the population most underrepresented in building health AI is most exposed to displacement by AI generally. What's missing?

  1. No audited statistic for women's share of the health-AI workforce specifically. Every workforce figure here is a proxy from a broader AI or health-tech population; UNESCO itself flags the data as "sparse, outdated, too broad, and often omits gender dimensions."
  2. No gender data for AI drug discovery or computational biology — the fastest-growing R&D application of AI has no audited representation figure.
  3. Founder gender within health-AI funding is unmeasured. Rock Health tracks the women's-health topic; PitchBook tracks founder gender across all sectors; neither isolates women-founded health-AI.
  4. Medtech-specific AI/R&D engineering gender data does not exist — only broad medtech (21% exec) and broad business-R&D (12% engineering) figures, both pre-2023.
  5. No intervention study measures representation gains in health-AI or medtech R&D. Every "what works" example below is from general women-in-STEM or global-health programs.

What works?

  • Female Global Scholars Program (global health research) — a mixed-methods evaluation reports the cohort produced 35 first-author papers, 12 oral presentations, 8 grants and 5 promotions: research-productivity gains, not just satisfaction scores (PMC, 2024). Tier 2.
  • HIGHER Women consortium mentoring — measured increases in peer-reviewed publications and conference presentations among participants (PMC, 2023). Tier 2.
  • CRA-W Grad Cohort Workshop (computing) — a formally evaluated intervention for retaining women in computing graduate programs; the model most transferable to AI R&D pipelines (PMC, 2017). Tier 2.
  • AI4ALL — 91% of participants report increased interest in AI careers and 78% plan to continue STEM education (program-reported, Tier 3).
  • Untested but frequently cited: female-only digital-literacy cohorts, femtech accelerators, and "women in medtech" ERGs — widely promoted, none with published comparison-group representation outcomes in this sector.
Recommended actions
  1. Read — Seyyed-Kalantari et al., Underdiagnosis bias of AI algorithms applied to chest radiographs in under-served patient populations (Nature Medicine, 2021): the clearest demonstration that under-representation in the data becomes under-diagnosis in the clinic.
  2. Share — the pairing: women are 34% of cardiovascular trial participants (JACC), yet the AI trained on that data is deployed to both sexes. It reframes diversity from an HR metric to a model-validity metric.
  3. Ask — put one question to your R&D or AI-governance team: "For our highest-stakes model, what is the sex balance of the training data, and have we measured performance separately for women?"
  4. Track — the female senior-author share in medical-AI literature (currently 27–37% by subfield): the leading, publicly measurable indicator of who will lead the next decade of clinical-AI R&D.
  5. Act — add a sex-disaggregated performance check to your next model-validation review and require at least one woman R&D scientist on the review. The decision layer (~14% of CTOs are women) is where the gap concentrates.
