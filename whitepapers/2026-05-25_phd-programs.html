Graduate school: women in AI/ML PhD programs

Eight consecutive years of progress at the doctoral level. Faculty hiring is the noisiest line. The advisor effect is the most robust finding in the literature.

Filed: 2026-05-25 Length: ~2,200 words Sources cited: 19 Tier 1: 12 · Tier 2: 6 · Tier 3: 1
25.9%
Women's share of CS/CE/I PhD recipients in North America, 2023–24 — the eighth consecutive annual rise, the strongest sustained positive trend in any women-in-AI dataset.
Executive summary
Women are 25.9% of 2023–24 CS/CE/I PhD recipients in North America (CRA Taulbee 2024) — the eighth consecutive annual rise. But the AI-specialty subset tells a sharper story: women have been under 19% of AI PhD graduates across the past decade, and the most-replicated finding in the academic literature — the advisor-gender effect — shows that female PhD students with female advisors persist longer, publish more, and place better. The single most consequential gap: no public dataset isolates AI-PhD completion rates by gender, so we know who enrolls and graduates but cannot count who silently leaves.

Eight straight years up: women's share of CS PhD recipients (N. America)
From 20.3% (2018–19) to 25.9% (2023–24), CRA Taulbee aggregate — the steepest stretch in the recorded series.
Source: CRA Taulbee Survey 2019, 2022, 2023, 2024 (US/Canada PhD-granting CS/CE/I departments). The 2023–24 release covers 157 of 314 departments (50% response rate). Women's share of enrolled doctoral students has also risen for eight straight years (21.6% in 2015–16 → 27.1% in 2023–24).

What's the number?

Enrolled CS PhD students who are female: 22.7% (CRA Taulbee 2023 Survey — 182 PhD-conferring departments). CS/CE/I PhD recipients who are female: 25.9% in 2023–24, up from 24.1% (2022–23) and 22.9% (2021–22). Eighth consecutive annual rise.

For the AI-specialty subset specifically: AI/ML is the single most popular PhD specialty in CS (>25% of doctorates where specialty is known), and women have been under 19% of AI PhD graduates across the 2013–2022 decade. Stanford HAI's annualized cut: 2021 AI PhD graduates were 21.3% female, up from 18.1% in 2011.

Broader CS PhDs are at 25.9% women and rising; AI-specialty PhDs are at ~21% and rising slower. The AI sub-field is less female than CS as a whole, not more.

What's the trend?

The faculty pipeline trend is bifurcated:

  • Women's share of new CS/CE/I faculty hires: 24.9% (2017) → 30.2% (2021) — improving.
  • But CRA Taulbee 2022–23 reported new tenure-track faculty hires fell from 25.9% to 20.0% women in one year — below the 22.9% female share of new PhDs. One bad year, but it broke the pattern.
  • Female faculty at top-18 universities whose primary research focus is AI: 16.1% (Stanford HAI 2024).

What's missing?

  1. No public dataset of AI-PhD completion rates by gender. CRA Taulbee tracks enrollment and degree production separately but does not publish cohort persistence. The single largest gap in the women-in-AI evidence base.
  2. No gender-disaggregated AI postdoc population. NSF GSS reports postdoc gender for all S&E but not AI as a subfield.
  3. No intersectional race × gender breakdown for CS/AI PhDs. CRA Taulbee 2023 reports CS PhDs awarded: Black 1.2% (18 recipients), Hispanic 2.1% (32 recipients) — gender cuts require NSF SED restricted-access microdata.
  4. No measured longitudinal placement outcomes for WiML or Rising Stars in EECS.
  5. Stanford, MIT, CMU, Berkeley do not publish program-specific AI PhD gender breakdowns in standardized form.
  6. 2024 Taulbee AI-specialty × gender cross-tab is not publicly summarized. Last published in 2019 ("Expanding the Pipeline," women = 13.4% of AI/ML PhD recipients, 2012–2018 aggregate).

What works?

Strongest measured-outcome intervention in the women-in-AI literature:

  • CRA-W Grad Cohort for Women. Master's-and-PhD-stage workshop. PNAS (PMC5222789): 67% of master's-stage attendees intended to pursue a PhD vs. 29% of matched non-participants; 71% first-author publication rate vs. 23% in 1–3 years post-workshop. Matched-cohort survey evidence — cleanest available.

Widely used, no rigorous published outcomes: Rising Stars in EECS (MIT, since 2012), WiML Workshop (NeurIPS-colocated, since 2006).

The advisor-effect bundle: three independent studies, one finding
Female PhD students with female peers and female advisors complete at higher rates, publish more, and place better. The literature treats this as robust.
Sources: Canaan & Mouganie (JHR); Gaule & Piacentini (Research Policy); Bostwick & Weinberg (JOLE / NBER). All three are peer-reviewed; first two are paywalled (preprint available); third is open-access.
Recommended actions
  1. Read — The CRA-W Grad Cohort evaluation (PMC5222789). Cleanest comparison-group evidence for a graduate-stage women-in-CS intervention.
  2. Share — The advisor-effect bundle (Canaan & Mouganie; Gaule & Piacentini; Bostwick & Weinberg). Three independent designs converging. Cleanest argument for increasing female faculty representation in AI programs as a persistence intervention.
  3. Ask — Of any AI PhD program: "What is your female-graduate enrollment %, and what % of your AI-specialty faculty are women?" The ratio tells you whether the program is set up to retain the women it admits.
  4. Track — The annual CRA Taulbee release. Watch the AI-specialty × gender cross-tab specifically — last published in 2019 and an update is overdue.
  5. Act — If you mentor a woman in an AI PhD, introduce her to at least one woman one career stage ahead of her before the academic year ends. The peer-effect literature says cohort-shaping is measurably consequential; the smallest unit of cohort-shaping is one introduction.
