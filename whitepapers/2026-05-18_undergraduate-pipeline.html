The pipeline: women in undergraduate CS and AI programs

22.6% today. 37.1% in 1984. The trajectory is reversible — the data shows it once was.

Filed: 2026-05-18 Length: ~2,000 words Sources cited: 17 Tier 1: 11 · Tier 2: 4 · Tier 3: 2
37.1% → 22.6%
Women's share of US CS bachelor's degrees: the 1984 peak (37.1%) to 2010 trough (17.6%) to today (22.6%). A 30-point swing that falsifies any "natural equilibrium" framing of women in computing.
Executive summary
Women earned 22.6% of US computer science bachelor's degrees in 2021–22 (NCES IPEDS) — less than the historical peak of 37.1% in 1983–84. The deepest finding in the trend data is that CS is one of the only STEM fields where women's share went down for nearly thirty years before recovering (trough of 17.6% in 2010–11). The single most actionable lever today is AP Computer Science Principles, which carries 34% female participation vs. AP CS A's 26% — the introductory course is where the pipeline widens or narrows, and the data is unambiguous about which version works.

What's the number?

In the United States, 22.6% of computer and information sciences bachelor's degrees were awarded to women in 2021–22 (NCES Digest of Education Statistics, Table 325.35). This is the canonical figure cited by virtually every downstream report. At PhD-granting CS departments specifically, CRA Taulbee 2024 reports 23.7%. The two figures bracket the true national rate within about one point.

There is no Tier 1 national statistic for women's share of AI-specific undergraduate concentrations. IPEDS CIP code 11.0102 ("Artificial Intelligence") was added recently; institutional reporting is sparse. Stanford HAI's AI Index uses CRA Taulbee AI-specialty PhD data as a proxy.

What's the trend?

The US trend is the story. Women's share of CS bachelor's degrees did not march upward over 50 years like most STEM fields. It rose, collapsed, and is slowly recovering.

The AP pipeline shows where momentum is now:

AP CS Principles vs. AP CS A — the introductory course is the bottleneck
CSP has held a ~9-point higher female-participation share than CS A every year since CSP launched in 2016–17.
Source: College Board newsroom releases, 2024. AP Computer Science Principles (CSP) 2023: 164,505 total exam-takers, 55,572 women. AP Computer Science A (CSA) 2023: 96,588 total, 24,147 women. CSA grew +7% YoY and +81% since 2017 in female participation.

What's missing?

  1. No standardized national data on women in AI-specific undergrad majors. IPEDS CIP code 11.0102 exists; institutional reporting too sparse for Tier 1 statistics.
  2. No national US attrition rate for women in CS undergrad. Single-institution figures circulate (Penn 13.6% vs. 2.5%; CACM 21% vs. 15%) but CRA Taulbee explicitly does not measure persistence.
  3. China CS undergrad gender data not openly published at national level.
  4. Race-by-gender CS bachelor's counts derivable but not published as a Tier 1 number. Most "Black women in CS" or "Latina in CS" figures circulate via Tier 2 compilations.
  5. AI4ALL and Kode With Klossy lack independent third-party evaluations comparable to AIR's Girls Who Code study.

What works?

Programs with rigorous independent evaluation:

  • Girls Who Code Summer Immersion. AIR quasi-experimental evaluation (March 2024, ERIC ED647331): participants +13.2 pp more likely to major in CS than propensity-weighted comparison group. Self-Paced Program: +11.5 pp. Self-reported alumni: 15× national CS-major rate; 16× for Black/Latina alumni.
  • BRAID Initiative. UCLA / Linda Sax (NSF Award 1525737): across 15 participating CS departments, 108% rise in women's computing enrollment over the BRAID period; women's growth outpaced overall growth at every institution.

Strong self-report, no independent evaluation: Black Girls CODE (Rockman 2017 case study), AI4ALL (88% alumni continuing CS/AI, 2021 cohort), Kode With Klossy.

The interventions that move the number are multi-year, departmental or curricular, and measured. Single-day events, sponsorship logos, and "Day for Girls" promotions show up in zero published evaluations as moving the major-completion rate.
Recommended actions
  1. Read — The AIR Girls Who Code evaluation (ERIC ED647331). Cleanest comparison-group evidence in the female-pipeline literature.
  2. Share — The 1984 → 2010 trough chart (37.1% → 17.6%). Most economical visual rebuttal to "girls just don't like CS."
  3. Ask — Of any high school in your network: "Does this school offer AP CS Principles, and what is the female enrollment ratio?" The answer doubles as a school-quality signal.
  4. Track — The annual NCES IPEDS Digest Table 325.35 release. Canonical longitudinal series for US CS-bachelor's gender share.
  5. Act — If you fund or sponsor any pipeline program, prioritize the ones with independent third-party evaluations (Girls Who Code, BRAID-affiliated departments). The opportunity cost of funding an unmeasured program is funding a measured one.
