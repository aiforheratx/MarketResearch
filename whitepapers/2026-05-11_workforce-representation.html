State of women in AI: representation across the workforce

A field with a 30% skill supply and a 22% role share — the gap is the story.

Filed: 2026-05-11 Length: ~2,000 words Sources cited: 16 Tier 1: 10 · Tier 2: 4 · Tier 3: 2
22%
Women's share of the global AI workforce, role-based (Stanford HAI 2024; corroborated by UNESCO 2024 and Interface EU 2024). Flat for a decade.
Executive summary
Women make up roughly 22% of the global AI workforce — a number that has barely moved in a decade despite ten years of public commitments. But the story is not flat: women's share of AI-skill-listing professionals on LinkedIn rose from ~22% (2016) to 30.5% (2024), even as their share of actual AI roles stayed near 22%. The field has more qualified women than it employs. The single number worth carrying into every conversation about women in AI is the leadership gap: under 14% of senior executive AI roles are held by women — and that is where action will pay the largest dividend.

The skill-vs-role gap is the field's signature problem
Women's share of LinkedIn AI-skill-listers exceeds women's share of held AI roles by 8 percentage points.
Sources: LinkedIn Economic Graph (skill supply, 2024); Stanford HAI AI Index 2024 + UNESCO 2024 (AI roles); UNESCO Women for Ethical AI 2024 (AI researchers); Interface EU AI's Missing Link 2024 (senior execs). All measures global, 2024.

What's the number?

The headline that most reports converge on is 22%: roughly one in five people working in AI globally is a woman. Three independent sources triangulate to this figure for 2024:

  • Stanford HAI AI Index 2024 (Chapter 8: Diversity) — 22% women across global AI roles, aggregating industry and academic data.
  • Interface EU's AI's Missing Link (2024) — same 22% figure across approximately 1.6 million identifiable AI professionals worldwide. Gender inferred from first names matched to census data.
  • UNESCO's Women for Ethical AI Outlook Study (October 2024) — decomposes the picture: 20% of technical roles at major ML companies, 12% of AI researchers, 18% of AI startup executives.

These are role-based measures. They count people holding AI jobs.

The supply of women with AI skills (~30%) exceeds the share of women holding AI roles (~22%) by 8–10 percentage points. The gap between skill and role is itself the most actionable finding in the data: the field has more qualified women than it employs.

A different number circulates in headlines: 30.5%. This is the LinkedIn Economic Graph figure for women's share of members who list AI engineering skills on their profile (2024, via WEF Global Gender Gap Report 2024; 75-country sample). It is a skill-supply measure, not a role measure. The two are not in conflict — they measure different things.

Applying the 22% role figure to Interface EU's 1.6M sample implies roughly 352,000 women globally in identified AI roles. No primary statistical body publishes a definitive absolute total.

What's the trend?

Two trend lines, one widening gap
Women's share of AI skills has risen ~8 pp since 2016. Women's share of AI roles has barely moved.
Sources: LinkedIn Economic Graph / WEF GGGR 2024 (skill supply); Stanford HAI AI Index 2023 + 2024 (roles). The skill-supply line measures women's % of LinkedIn members listing AI engineering skills; the role line measures women's share of held AI roles.

Women's share of new AI PhDs (North America): 18.1% in 2011 → 21.3% in 2021 (Stanford HAI AI Index). Direction: improving at roughly 0.5–1 pp per year. At this rate, parity in AI PhDs arrives around 2050.

Women's share of AI engineering skill-listers (LinkedIn): ~22% (2016) → 30.5% (2024) → 29.4% (2025). Rising for nearly a decade, plateaued in 2025. Nesta has flagged Europe's share specifically declined (Germany's gap widened 4 pp).

Women in AI roles (the role-based measure): essentially flat at ~22% over the same window. The supply rose; the role share did not.

What's missing?

Six evidence gaps the data does not answer today:

  1. No primary source publishes women's share of Principal / Staff / Distinguished AI engineer roles industry-wide. The closest proxy is Russell Reynolds' "22% across product/engineering/science leadership" cut across 39 AI organizations.
  2. No 2023+ figure for Black women's share of AI technical roles. The most-cited 3% statistic is from 2017 NCWIT and is not AI-specific. Women of color are 31% of workers in the 15 most AI-vulnerable US jobs (National Partnership 2024) — the absence of measurement at the top is conspicuous.
  3. No global absolute headcount of women in AI from any primary statistical body.
  4. AI startup founder/funding data are fragmented. UNESCO cites 0.7% of AI VC capital to female-led; WEF cites a 31.7% EU-wide gap — different definitions, no consistent global series.
  5. Mid-career attrition from AI roles by gender is not tracked by NSF NCSES, BLS, or Eurostat.
  6. No cross-cut by AI sub-discipline. Women's share of generative AI vs. classical ML vs. AI safety is absent from every major source.

What works?

Programs with measured outcomes (not just stated intentions):

  • Girls Who Code Summer Immersion Program. Independent AIR evaluation: participants +13.2 pp more likely to major in a CS-related field than propensity-weighted comparison group. Alumni at 15× national CS-major rate; 16× for Black/Latina alumni. The strongest measured-outcome intervention in the literature.
  • Salesforce equal-pay audits. Cumulative ~$10.3M in adjustments by 2019; $5.6M in 2022 alone. Continuous annualized auditing moves the number; one-time audits do not.
  • AnitaB.org Top Companies for Women Technologists. Seven consecutive years of rising tech-women hire share at participating firms. Paused in 2024, interrupting the only multi-year industry benchmark.
Recommended actions
  1. Read — UNESCO's Women for Ethical AI Outlook Study (Oct 2024). Single most carefully grounded global view in the literature; disaggregates technical / research / executive clearly.
  2. Share — The 30.5% AI skills vs. 22% AI roles gap. Most economical way to puncture the "we can't find women" hiring argument.
  3. Ask — In your org: "What is our director-and-above gender ratio in AI specifically, and what was it last year?" If the answer is "we don't track AI separately," that is the answer.
  4. Track — The annual CRA Taulbee Survey release (typically May). Only Tier 1 longitudinal source for the AI-PhD pipeline.
  5. Act — Commit in your next hiring loop to proactively source from at least one named pipeline org (WiML, Black in AI, LatinX in AI, AnitaB.org Top Companies alumni). The 22% number does not move by accident.
