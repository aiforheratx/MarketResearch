Entry-level AI roles: first job in AI/ML

A 12-point gap in who is told to use AI tools, at the very moment when AI fluency becomes a promotion lever.

Filed: 2026-06-01 Length: ~2,100 words Sources cited: 19 Tier 1: 9 · Tier 2: 4 · Tier 3: 6
21% vs 33%
Entry-level women report being encouraged by their manager to use AI tools at 21%; men at 33% (McKinsey/LeanIn Women in the Workplace 2025). The most consequential new data point of 2025.
Executive summary
The most striking new finding in 2025 is from McKinsey/LeanIn's Women in the Workplace 2025: entry-level women report 21% manager encouragement to use AI tools vs. 33% for men — a 12-point gap at the very moment when AI fluency is becoming a promotion lever. The classic "broken rung" persists too: for every 100 men hired or promoted into entry-level-or-manager roles, only 81 women are. And the most-cited entry-level women-in-tech retention statistic — 50% of women in tech leave by age 35 (AnitaB.org) — has no AI-specific equivalent in any public dataset. The data we have about women's first AI jobs is dominated by what we do not measure.

What's the number?

Women's share of global AI engineering talent: 30.5% in 2024, up from 29.3% in 2016 (LinkedIn Economic Graph via OECD.AI). At named major AI employers (US technical roles, 2024): Google 27.2%; Microsoft 27.2%; NVIDIA 19% (total, 2022 most recent). OpenAI, Anthropic, DeepMind, Meta AI do not break out AI-research-role gender publicly in a comparable standardized form.

EU ICT specialists: 19.5% women (Eurostat 2024). Range 13.0% (Czechia) to 27.6% (Estonia). Singapore is the global leader on LinkedIn's measure at ~35%.

India: NASSCOM/Deloitte 2024 flag a decline in women's representation at the junior professional level, despite 96% of Indian women in tech reporting intent to build AI skills. No clean single-figure share is publicly disclosed.

What's the trend?

The broken rung — and the new 2025 AI-encouragement gap
Two structural mechanisms women face at the start of their AI career. The first is six years old and has barely moved. The second is a year old and shaping who gets the next promotion.
81. AI-tool encouragement, entry-level "My manager encouraged me to use AI tools" McKinsey/LeanIn Women in the Workplace 2025 21% Women 33% Men 12 pp gap
Left: McKinsey/LeanIn Women in the Workplace 2024 — 281 companies, 10M+ employees. The number was 79 in 2018; 81 in 2024 — a 2-point gain over six years. Right: McKinsey/LeanIn Women in the Workplace 2025 — survey of ~9,000 employees across 120+ North American firms.

What's missing?

  1. No public AI/ML-specific hiring funnel by gender. LinkedIn and Indeed publish stock figures; neither publishes a flow funnel (applied → interviewed → offered → accepted).
  2. AI residency programs do not disclose demographics. Google AI Residency, Meta AI Residency, OpenAI Residency, Anthropic Research Programs — all explicitly recruit underrepresented applicants, none publicly disclose composition or conversion-to-FT rates.
  3. No primary entry-level AI hiring share by gender for China, Brazil, or any African country.
  4. No published 12-month retention rate specific to entry-level women in AI/ML. The 50%-leave-by-age-35 AnitaB.org stat is all of tech, not AI.
  5. CS-degree → AI-role conversion rate by gender (1–2 year outlook) not published by NSF NCSES or NCES.
  6. AnitaB.org Top Companies benchmark paused in 2024. The only multi-year benchmarking series of entry-level technical women at named employers is interrupted.

What works?

  • Code2040 Fellows. Black and Latinx CS undergrads → tech internships → full-time conversion. ~90% return-offer rate (twice industry baseline ~45–50%); 100% subsequently work in tech.
  • AI4ALL. High-school → college pipeline. 88% of alumni pursuing CS/AI; 50% obtained an internship (2021 cohort). Self-reported.
  • AnitaB.org Top Companies (paused 2024): women's tech-hire share rose for 7 consecutive years through 2023; attrition reached near-parity with men. The pause is itself a data event.
The pattern across the strongest programs is identical to the pipeline finding: multi-year, structurally embedded, and benchmarked. Single events — even high-profile ones — do not show up in entry-level outcome data.
Recommended actions
  1. Read — McKinsey/LeanIn Women in the Workplace 2025 (specifically the AI-encouragement section). The 21% vs. 33% figure is the single most important new data point in entry-level women-in-AI for 2025.
  2. Share — The AI-tool encouragement gap (21% vs. 33%) with a question: "What is the gender split of who in our org has been told to use AI tools by their manager?"
  3. Ask — Of any company doing AI hiring: "What is your published 12-month retention rate for entry-level technical women, vs. men?" If no such number exists, that is the lever to push on — measured retention is the prerequisite for fixing it.
  4. Track — Code2040 annual fellows-outcomes release. Strongest measured intersectional intervention with public numbers.
  5. Act — If you manage anyone, make encouragement to use AI tools an explicit conversation in 1:1s with women you manage. The 12-point gap is most likely driven by the absence of that conversation, not by a refusal to have it.
