The broken rung — promotion from IC to first management

In technical roles the first promotion is twice as broken as the corporate average. The 2025 "repair" was real — but only for some women.

Filed: 2026-06-15 Length: ~2,400 words Sources cited: 10 Tier 1: 5 · Tier 2: 3 · Tier 3: 2

Executive summary
In technical roles, only 52 women are promoted to manager for every 100 men — barely half the all-industry rate of 86 (McKinsey/LeanIn 2021, still the most recent technical cut). The all-industry rung finally moved in 2025 — 93 women per 100 men, up from 81 in 2024 — but the gain was selective: women of color stayed at 74, and Black women at 60, unchanged. The broken rung is the mechanism behind every senior-level gap this knowledge base has logged — and no source has measured it inside AI specifically at all.

What's the number?

The broken rung is the first promotion from individual contributor to manager — the step where the pipeline first leaks and the one that sets a ceiling on everything above it. The defining number for this topic is not the all-industry figure but the technical-roles cut: 52 women promoted to manager for every 100 men, against 86 across all roles (McKinsey/LeanIn Women in the Workplace 2021). In technical functions the rung is roughly twice as broken as the corporate average — and 2021 is the most recent technical-specific number McKinsey has published.

The all-industry rung, by contrast, is freshly measured and — for the first time in this knowledge base — moved the right way: 93 women per 100 men in 2025, up from 81 in 2024. The representation pyramid shows the compounding cost: women are 48% of entry-level workers, 39% of managers, and just 29% of the C-suite (2025). In STEM functions the leak is steeper still — 29% entry → 24.4% managerial → 12.2% C-suite (2024). A field that starts near parity ends near one-in-eight, and it does most of its leaking at the very first rung.

The 81→93 jump looks like progress — until you disaggregate. Women of color sat at 74 per 100 in 2025, and Black women at 60, both essentially unchanged. The rung is not repairing. It is repairing selectively.

What's the trend?

All-industry rung, recent series (per 100 men): 86 all-roles / 52 technical (2021) → 87 (2023) → 81 (2024) → 93 (2025). McKinsey has reported the rung two ways across years — promotions-only and hired-plus-promoted — so absolute levels shift with the metric; the 2024→2025 comparison above is like-for-like. The read: improving on average, stalled for women of color, and roughly twice as bad in technical roles where it has not been measured in four years.

Why the rung is the binding constraint. Because men outnumber women at the first manager level, there are structurally fewer women eligible for every promotion above it. Fix entry-level hiring parity and nothing else, and the pipeline still never reaches parity — the rung re-introduces the deficit every year. This is the mechanism behind the <14% women in senior executive AI roles (Interface EU 2024), the 16.1% on tenure-track AI faculty (Stanford HAI 2024), and the executive $0.69-per-$1.00 pay cliff from Week 5. The pay cliff at the top is, in large part, a promotion cliff at the bottom.

The AI accelerant. Entry-level women are told to use AI tools by managers far less than men (21% vs. 33%, 2025), and men use AI daily about 22% more (LeanIn/WEF 2025). If AI fluency becomes a promotion criterion — and in AI-building orgs it will — an unequal head start at the IC level converts directly into an unequal promotion rate at the rung.

What's missing?

  1. No AI-specific broken-rung number exists. The closest proxy — the 2021 technical-roles figure (52 per 100) — bundles all engineering and product, is four years old, and predates the generative-AI hiring boom. No manager-promotion rate by gender has been published for AI engineers, ML scientists, or research staff. This is the central gap.
  2. The technical-roles cut has not been refreshed since 2021. The all-industry rung is annual; the technical breakout is not. We cannot say whether it tracked the 81→93 improvement or stayed stuck.
  3. No intersectional (race × gender) promotion data inside AI or technical roles. We have women-of-color rung numbers economy-wide (74; Black women 60) and technical numbers for all women (52) — never the two crossed.
  4. No named AI lab publishes first-line-manager representation or promotion rates by gender. Diversity reports give headcount, not promotion velocity.
  5. "Manager" is defined inconsistently. In IC-heavy AI orgs the first rung is often a staff/principal IC promotion, not a people-management move — and no dataset distinguishes the two by gender. The AI-relevant rung may not be the one McKinsey measures.
  6. Sponsorship is measured as access, not outcome. We know 31% of entry-level women have a sponsor vs. 45% of men — but no public dataset ties a named program to a measured change in women's promotion rate at the rung.

What works?

  • Structured, debiased promotion processes — the strongest signal. Of 40 leaders McKinsey interviewed, only 2 reported organizations without a broken-rung problem — both Fortune 100 tech firms with heavily structured, committee-based promotion systems (defined criteria, calibration, bias checks, panel decisions). Discretion at the rung is where bias enters; removing it is what closes the gap.
  • The three enablers (McKinsey/LeanIn technical framework): equitable access to skill-building and high-visibility projects; structured promotion processes; and a sponsorship (not mentorship) culture — advocacy in the room where promotions are decided.
  • Sponsorship parity erases the ambition gap — measured. Women want promotions nearly as much as men (80% vs. 86%, 2025) yet are 13% less likely to get one. LeanIn 2025: when women receive equal career support, the promotion-ambition gap disappears entirely. The deficit is sponsorship, not ambition — and it is the most directly fixable enabler.
  • The business case is quantified. The most gender-diverse leadership teams are 48% more likely to financially outperform the least diverse (McKinsey Diversity Matters Even More, 2023). The rung is a measured performance cost, not only an equity one.
Recommended actions
  1. Read — McKinsey/LeanIn's Repairing the broken rung … for women in technical roles. The only source that isolates the technical rung (52 per 100) and names the three enablers with a clear mechanism.
  2. Share — The split: the rung rose to 93 per 100 overall in 2025, but women of color stayed at 74 and Black women at 60. The cleanest way to puncture "the broken rung is fixing itself."
  3. Ask — Of your org or board: "What is our promotion rate to first-level manager, by gender and race, for technical roles — and is the decision made by a calibrated committee or by individual managers?" If it isn't tracked or the call is discretionary, that is the answer.
  4. Track — The annual Women in the Workplace release for the all-industry rung, and watch for any refresh of the technical-roles cut — the single number this knowledge base most needs updated.
  5. Act — If you sit in a promotion calibration, sponsor, don't mentor: name a specific woman IC and advocate for her readiness in the room where it's decided. Sponsorship parity is the one enabler shown to erase the ambition gap outright.
